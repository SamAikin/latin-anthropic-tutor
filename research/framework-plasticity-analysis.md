# Framework Plasticity Analysis

How well the stateful AI-tutor framework developed for this Latin study project would transfer
to other languages. Generated 2026-07-11 via research + web search, then reviewed against
SLA literature. See README.md for the full framework description.

## Summary Table

| Language | What Would Work Well | What Would Work Poorly | Special Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **German** | Near-perfect fit. German has an enormous attested corpus, excellent frequency dictionaries, and strong Anki deck ecosystems. The framework's read-for-comprehension emphasis works well since German grammar is highly regular and morphological rules surface quickly. Generated passages are reliable and easily verifiable. | The "no ergonomic assist" rule in readings will hit early learners hard: German case endings are non-trivial and compound nouns can be genuinely opaque without aids. The model convention (no helps in reading) is strict but manageable. | German is a living language — late-stage goal should eventually include native-produced audio/video input outside this framework to reinforce the aural channel. For a read-language goal only, the framework works cleanly from day one. |
| **French** | Highly compatible. Massive reading corpus, the best frequency word lists of any Romance language, and copious attested literary sources for advanced reading practice. The model's French is very reliable, lowering the closed-evaluative-loop risk. | French orthography is famously opaque (silent letters, liaisons): the "no annotation" rule in readings means learners must internalize a spelling-to-sound system that even native children struggle with. For a pure reading goal this is less acute but still a complication. | French is a living language with rich literary tradition (Montaigne, Pascal, Proust) — the framework's provenance-and-historical-text approach maps naturally. Good opportunity for genuine historical text early. |
| **Spanish** | Excellent fit. Highly regular morphology, extremely transparent orthography, vast corpus, and the best second-language acquisition research base of any language. Frequency lists are mature. The 98% coverage threshold is easiest to hit in Spanish because high-frequency words cover an unusually large proportion of text. | Verb aspect and subjunctive mood can require more drilling than the one-concept-per-lesson format initially accommodates — lessons may need discipline not to overload these chapters. | For an advanced learner or a reading-only goal, the framework is nearly ideal. Consider pivoting early to Golden Age Spanish prose (Cervantes, Gracián) once fundamentals are in place — the framework's historical-text handling suits this well. |
| **Russian** | Strong fit for a motivated learner. Excellent frequency dictionaries and Anki deck ecosystems exist. Russian's rich morphology means the stateful learner model becomes genuinely valuable: tracking declension mastery across 6 cases is exactly the "gradient recalculation" use case. | The Cyrillic script is a prerequisite before any reading can begin — it's not covered by the framework as designed (no phonics/script-acquisition track exists in the templates). The closed-evaluative-loop risk is higher: Russian morphological nuance at the intermediate level is where LLM errors are less obvious to a novice. | Script onboarding should be treated as Week 0 content before the syllabus proper begins. Also: Russian has aspectual pairs (perfective/imperfective verb pairs) for almost every verb — this needs explicit tracking in the learner model and deserves its own skill-level checkpoints. |
| **Ukrainian** | The framework works in principle, with the same strengths as Russian: inflected morphology benefits from a stateful learner model, and frequency-weighted vocab acquisition is well-suited to a language with a smaller high-frequency core than English. | Significantly fewer mature Anki decks and frequency dictionaries than Russian. The model's Ukrainian is less reliable than its Russian — the closed-evaluative-loop risk is meaningfully higher and grading errors harder to spot. Historical attested text options are more limited for a late-stage literary reading goal. | Cyrillic onboarding same as Russian. Also: Ukrainian and Russian share vocabulary but differ in phonology and some morphology — a Russian-background learner will have transfer interference. The learner model should track these specifically. The political and cultural dimension (intentional differentiation from Russian) may shape which texts to foreground. |
| **Modern Greek** | Good fit. Greek has a phonetically regular orthography (once the alphabet is learned), a living speaker community, a good attested corpus from multiple eras, and solid Anki resources. The framework's historical text capability is a genuine asset here — the bridge from Modern back to Classical Greek is motivating and well-documented. | The alphabet is a prerequisite (same problem as Cyrillic). Modern Greek has significant diglossia history (Katharevousa vs Demotic) — the framework needs an explicit stance on which register is the target to avoid the syllabus drifting between them. | The strongest special case for Modern Greek: if the longer-term goal includes Ancient Greek, a Modern Greek foundation is not the shortest path (the grammars diverge enough that it can create interference). Flag this in the North Star section. |
| **Ancient Greek** | Possibly the best fit of any language on this list after Latin itself. Ancient Greek is explicitly a read language (no pronunciation community), has the same "no living speaker" profile that shaped the Latin design, an enormous attested corpus via Perseus/TLG, and mature pedagogical traditions. The Socratic lesson format has a certain appropriateness here. | The morphological complexity (more intricate verb system than Latin, three grammatical genders, dual number, optative mood) means the learner model will need to track more granular grammar checkpoints. The no-annotation-in-readings rule may need to flex earlier than in Latin because Greek accentuation is phonemically meaningful and entirely absent from ancient manuscripts. | The framework as designed maps almost 1:1. Consider a parallel track for reading different dialects (Homeric, Attic, Koine) as an analog to the Latin style-comparison notes already in the README. Strong provenance ecosystem (Perseus) makes WebSearch verification more reliable than for Latin. |
| **Modern Hebrew** | Works well for motivated learners. Hebrew morphology (root-and-pattern system) rewards exactly the kind of grammatical-pattern tracking the learner model does well. The root system also makes high-frequency vocab acquisition unusually efficient — understanding 500 roots unlocks thousands of derived words. | Modern Hebrew is written in an unvoweled abjad in everyday use — native-like reading requires the learner to infer vowels from morphological and contextual knowledge simultaneously. The no-annotation reading rule collides directly with this: below an intermediate level, unvoweled reading practice is closer to guessing than comprehension. Even native children read vowelized texts for the first two years of school. | The framework needs an explicit policy for the vowel question: vowelized texts in early readings (analogous to macrons in Latin vocab lists), transitioning to unvoweled text as a tracked milestone in the learner model. Without this, the reading track will stall or produce false confidence. |
| **Portuguese** | High compatibility, essentially parallel to Spanish. Very good frequency resources, transparent orthography, and a strong Anki ecosystem. The attested literary corpus (Camões, Pessoa, Eça de Queirós) makes historical and literary text practice tractable. | The European vs. Brazilian dialect split needs an explicit decision up front — they differ in vocabulary, phonology, and some grammar, and the framework shouldn't drift between them. The model defaults toward Brazilian Portuguese which may or may not match the learner's goal. | Lock in a dialect target in the North Star from day one. Otherwise the framework functions cleanly and would require minimal adaptation from the Latin design. |
| **Klingon** | The stateful tutor model is well-suited to Klingon's unusual grammar (OVS word order, elaborate verb suffix chains) — tracking these through a structured lesson sequence is exactly what this framework does well. ~3,000–4,000 attested vocabulary words exist, enough for structured syllabus planning. | Klingon has no authentic unadapted historical text in the sense the framework uses the concept — every "attested" passage originates in a TV production script, not a literary tradition. The provenance framework becomes awkward; there is no equivalent to Cicero to aim at. Daily reading would necessarily be almost entirely generated, keeping the closed-evaluative-loop risk permanently high. The "forever North Star" (acquisition *through* the language as a window into a culture/history) has no natural analog. | The framework's bones work; its soul doesn't quite fit. Treat it as an intellectual exercise in constructed grammar, not cultural acquisition. The Klingon Language Institute's texts (Hamlet in Klingon, etc.) are the closest to "historical source material" this framework can use. |
| **Tolkien's Elvish (Quenya/Sindarin)** | The framework's structure is appealing for Quenya — it is the most complete of the Elvish languages, with a documented grammar, thousands of vocabulary items, and decades of scholarly work by Tolkienian linguists. The one-concept-per-lesson cadence suits a language that is studied rather than spoken. | Tolkien himself acknowledged the languages were incomplete — vocabulary and idiomatic grammar have deliberate gaps that no amount of planning can fill. There is no sufficient corpus to support 70 words/week for 4+ weeks without invention, and the framework's provenance requirement becomes unsolvable: there is no ground truth to WebSearch-verify against, and the distinction between attested Tolkien text and fan reconstruction is deeply contested. | This framework would need a fundamental redesign of the vocab pipeline and the provenance model. Sindarin is significantly less complete than Quenya and would break the framework faster. For a motivated Tolkienian, this could be adapted as a scholarly project (tracking what is attested vs. reconstructed explicitly) but should not be attempted as a standard language acquisition program. |

## Cross-Cutting Observations

The framework maps most cleanly onto **read languages with large attested corpora and no
living speaker standard** — Ancient Greek being the closest analog to Latin. It maps well
onto modern European languages with transparent orthographies (Spanish, Portuguese, French,
German) and degrades gracefully where the main gap is script acquisition.

The two places it needs the most structural surgery are:

1. **Abjad writing systems** (Hebrew, and by extension Arabic) — the no-annotation reading
   rule needs a staged vowelization policy, analogous to macrons in Latin vocab lists,
   transitioning to unvoweled text as an explicit learner-model milestone.

2. **Constructed or incomplete languages** (Klingon and Elvish) — the provenance and corpus
   assumptions break down architecturally, not just in degree. The vocab pipeline and
   provenance model would need fundamental redesign.
