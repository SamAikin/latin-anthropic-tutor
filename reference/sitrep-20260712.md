Sit down, young man. You have asked for an honest accounting, and Cicero did not achieve the consulship by flattering his mentees. ## VOCAB

This is your weakest flank, and you should know that I knew it before you proved it. The diagnostic showed your verbs failing you where your nouns held — and now a pattern sharpens: **you trust strangers because they resemble your cousins.** *Magister* became "magistrate." *Exspecto* became "expect." *Consilium* became "consensus." *Vocabit* drifted toward "speaks" because it whispered of *vox*. Four incidents make a habit, not a coincidence. Hear me: when a Latin word resembles an English one, that resemblance is a *hypothesis*, to be tried against the sentence — not evidence, and never a verdict.

Yet I will grant you this, and grant it grudgingly because you have earned it: you added *surgo*, *tot*, *nam*, *mox*, *cotidie* to your own drill without being told. A man who arms himself before the drillmaster orders it will survive his first campaign.

## GRAMMAR

Your case system returned from exile fast — genitives clean, the neuter rule mostly firm, and your handling of ambiguous forms has gone from a watched weakness to a genuine strength in a single day. You named both readings of *poetae* unprompted. That habit of holding two readings open until context collapses one — that, young man, is not a beginner's habit. Guard it.

Two marks against you. First, **tense discipline buckles under load**: *veniet* and *vocabit* — plain futures, and *mox* standing beside them like a herald announcing them — came out as presents. The tense lesson has not yet been held, so I record this in pencil, not ink. Second, the vocative you omitted from *both* declension write-outs — not because you don't know it (you produced *Domine* correctly the same day) but because you did not finish what was asked. A quaestor who executes seven clauses of an eight-clause decree has executed none of it. This is your only *recurring* fault, and it is not a Latin fault. Fix it in the habit, not the grammar.

## CONSTRUCTIONS

*Hic... Illic.* You stumbled on this pair twice in one reading — once venially, once mortally, where "here the Roman people conducts its life" became something about giving lives. One small adverb hijacked an entire sentence. Learn its faces: *hic* the pointer and *hic* the place are distinguished by their company.

But now the jewel, and I polish it before I hand it over because it did not come cheap: you inferred *ius dicit* — the praetor's *iurisdictio* itself — from context, unprompted, and *verba facit* besides. Meeting an idiom you have never studied and reasoning your way to its technical sense is precisely the muscle that reading Latin — my Latin — will demand of you for years. That instinct cannot be taught. You brought it with you.

On graded prose your comprehension already flickers toward A2 — the Forum reading was understood *in situ*, not decoded word-by-word — and that is the number that matters for your purposes.

The only drag on your progress at present is your own haste: the slips (*filiam* as "son," *bella* gone singular) all occur under load, when speed outruns care.
