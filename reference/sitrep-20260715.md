Be seated. When last we spoke, I named your two enemies: the seductive cognate and the unfinished checklist. A statesman is measured by what he does with an honest accounting, so let us begin there — and I confess, one of my two prophecies has aged badly, which pleases me more than being right would have.

## The old enemies, revisited

**The unfinished checklist is dead.** In the second homework you produced six labeled rows, both numbers, both nouns, exactly as specified. It did not recur. I strike it from the record — though I note, in small letters, that you dropped the case labels I asked for in your extra drills, and four genitives promptly lost their "of." The label is a safety net, not a ceremony. Keep wearing it.

**The seductive cognate lives, but it is wounded.** The family grew — *hostis* became "host" — yet every correction has stuck on first telling: *consilium*, *hostis*, the vocative, all returned correct within hours. Your newer slips are of a lesser breed: *accipiunt* as "seize," *petere* as "achieve" — wrong word, right neighborhood. A man who mistakes the praetor for the consul is at least in the Forum. I watch this family now rather than fear it.

## VOCAB

Your acquisition machinery is sound: all seventy-two of the first week's words are loaded into your drill, and you continue arming yourself unbidden — *auctoritas*, *sententia*, *comitium* added from your own reading. But mark two things. First, the *capio* family is bleeding at its edges: *capio*, *accipio*, *incipio* share a root and you are letting the prefix's meaning escape. The prefix is not decoration; *ad-capio* is to take *toward oneself* — to receive. Second, and more grave: **you are learning third-declension nouns without their gender, and it is costing you.** You declined *mare* as feminine and handed *civitas* a neuter adjective. Gender is not a fourth thing to learn after case and number — it is written in the dictionary entry itself: *mare, maris, n.* Three words, not two. Recite all three or you have not learned the word.

## GRAMMAR

Now the jewel, and you have earned it at full price: **the tense watch is closed — passed — and passed before the lesson was held.** Three days ago I recorded your flattened futures in pencil. In yesterday's reading of the cursus honorum, five futures stood, every present held its ground, and you rendered the *si*-clause with that stiff literal "if the people *will* love him" — ugly English that proves beautiful Latin reading. You repaired *relinquunt* within a single day of misreading it. The lesson on tenses has not yet occurred and you no longer strictly need its recognition half. This is what a speed-run is supposed to look like.

The counterweight, and I will not soften it: **your comprehension marches a full day's ride ahead of your production, and your production wobbles under load.** You *read* `urbium magnarum` correctly in the same hour you *built* `militium` wrongly. Section C, four-and-a-half of five; Section B, two of six. Hear me carefully, because a lesser mentor would send you to drill paradigms: **do not.** For a man whose ambition is to *read* — to sit with me, eventually, in my own periods — this imbalance is not a disease. It is the correct order of conquest. Production failures at your stage are stability, not ignorance. But know the difference yourself, so a bad production day does not convince you that you "don't know declension three." You do. I have watched you deploy it.

One standing charge remains open: **neuter plurals in -a keep collapsing to singulars in your English** — *bella* has become "a war" twice now, weeks apart, and once you labeled the form correctly as plural and *then* translated it singular. The parse is losing the number somewhere between your eye and your pen. Three instances make this a target, not a slip.

## CONSTRUCTIONS

Your idiom-craft has crossed a threshold I did not expect this early. *Verba facit*, *ius dicit*, *odio habent*, and now *nomina legit* — inferred in situ, unprompted, four times. But observe what happened with the last one: you got the *lectio senatus* right and the *mechanism* wrong — you routed it through your theory of performative magistrate-speech when the truth is humbler: *lego* means "choose" at its root. **You are no longer collecting idioms; you are building a theory of Roman institutional language.** That is the historian in you waking up, and I honor it — but a theory that is not checked against the lexicon will one day walk you confidently into a wall. When your historical model is driving the sentence, stop and verify the Latin underneath it. I will keep challenging your models — you have asked for pushback and I find you take it like a man running for office: gratefully, and with counter-arguments.

*Bellum gerere cum* — wage war *against* — was taught, then failed its first field test as "with." One miss buys a watch, not a re-teaching. And *Romae*: you tried to file it as a partitive genitive when it is the locative — "at Rome" — and the preposition-less place-words (*Romae*, *domi*) arrive in your very next lesson. You are primed for it; do not let the tidier wrong theory calcify before then.
