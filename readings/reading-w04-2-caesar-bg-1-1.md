# Daily Reading — w04-2

## Provenance and Citations

**ADAPTED HISTORICAL TEXT (lightly).** Source: Caesar, *De Bello Gallico* 1.1, verified
against The Latin Library text (thelatinlibrary.com, retrieved 2026-07-11). The most
famous opening in Latin prose, published in the 50s BC — genuine late-republic Latin by
a genuine late-republic author. Read it critically as well as grammatically: this is
the conqueror of Gaul explaining to Roman readers why the Gauls needed conquering. The
ethnography (soft southerners corrupted by imports, hardy northerners toughened by war)
is Caesar's framing, not neutral fact.

Changes from the original: the gerundive phrase "ad effeminandos animos" (not yet
taught) is recast as "quae animos molles faciunt"; the detailed geography of the three
parts (the final third of the chapter) is omitted. Everything else is Caesar verbatim —
including the indirect statement pattern in "appellantur" clauses and the pure
indicative narration.

## Reading

Gallia est omnis divisa in partes tres, quarum unam incolunt Belgae, aliam Aquitani,
tertiam qui ipsorum lingua Celtae, nostra Galli appellantur. Hi omnes lingua,
institutis, legibus inter se differunt. Gallos ab Aquitanis Garumna flumen, a Belgis
Matrona et Sequana dividit.

Horum omnium fortissimi sunt Belgae, propterea quod a cultu atque humanitate provinciae
longissime absunt, minimeque ad eos mercatores saepe commeant atque ea important quae
animos molles faciunt; proximique sunt Germanis, qui trans Rhenum incolunt, quibuscum
continenter bellum gerunt.

Qua de causa Helvetii quoque reliquos Gallos virtute praecedunt, quod fere cotidianis
proeliis cum Germanis contendunt, cum aut suis finibus eos prohibent aut ipsi in eorum
finibus bellum gerunt.
