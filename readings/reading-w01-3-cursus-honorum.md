## Evaluation

**Overall: comprehended, and the headline is TENSE DISCIPLINE — passed, decisively, and
a day early.** This reading carried five futures (`erit` ×3, `amabit`, plus `erit` in the
si-clause) mixed in with presents, and you rendered every one of them future and every
present present — *before* the tense-system lesson (w01-3), which you haven't taken yet.
In reading w01-1 you flattened `veniet`/`vocabit` to present; that error is gone. Extra
credit hiding in your literalism: "if the people **will** love him" — Latin uses the
future in both halves of that conditional where English idiom uses a present ("if the
people love him"). Your slightly stiff English is direct evidence you saw the tense. That
is exactly the kind of awkward-but-accurate rendering this exercise is for.

**Also: the `exercitus` callback.** In w01-2 you flagged `exercitus` as ambiguously
singular/plural. Here (`Consul exercitus ducit`) you wrote "armies" — and this time the
grammar *forces* it: `consul` is the subject, so `exercitus` must be object, and the
accusative singular would be `exercitum`. The only possible parse is accusative plural.
You landed on the right reading of the exact form you flagged two days ago.

**The one correction that matters — your `Romae nemo` question, because you asked to
internalize the wrong pattern:** `Romae` here is **not** a partitive genitive; it is the
**locative** — "at Rome, no one can immediately be consul" (you dropped it from your
translation entirely). Two things to hold at once:

1. **Your pattern IS real Latin — keep it.** `nemo Gallorum` "none of the Gauls" is a
   genuine partitive genitive and worth internalizing. With `nemo` it takes a genitive
   *plural of persons*: nemo Romanorum, nemo vestrum.
2. **But that's not what this sentence does.** Names of cities and small islands keep a
   fossilized locative case: `Romae` = "at Rome," `Corinthi` = "at Corinth," `domi` = "at
   home." The form is spelled like a genitive/dative, which is the trap. The tell:
   "no one of Rome" would want people to partition (`nemo Romanorum`), and a bare city
   name with `nemo` sitting at the head of a sentence is overwhelmingly a scene-setter.
   Locatives are w01-4 territory (prepositions and case idioms), so this was ahead of
   your kit — flagging it rather than silently guessing was the right move.

**Smaller corrections:**
- **"Marcus the Young"** — `iuvenis Romanus` is the predicate: "Marcus **is a young
  Roman**." Reading `Marcus iuvenis` as a name-plus-epithet unit is a reasonable instinct
  (Romans did that — Cato Maior/Minor), but `est` needs a predicate and "the Young" would
  leave `Romanus est` saying only "is Roman."
- **`accipiunt` → "seize"** — `accipio` (ad + capio) is "receive, accept." The sentence's
  point is the opposite of seizing: this honor is *conferred* — only great men are
  *given* it (by the people, per the previous sentence). Watch the capio family:
  capio = take, accipio = receive, incipio = begin. `capio` is on your week-1 Anki list.
- **`petere` → "achieve" (twice: `honores petere vult`, `honores petunt`)** — `peto` is
  "seek, strive for, run for," not the attainment. This matters doubly here because
  `honores petere` is THE technical idiom for standing for office: a candidacy is a
  `petitio` (whence English "petition"), and the man in the whitened toga doing it is a
  `candidatus`. The final sentence's whole point is the *pursuit*: "the Romans seek their
  honors through many years and many offices" — some sought and never achieved.
- **`fortuna bona erit`** — your translation "he will be lucky" is fine smoothing, but
  your bracketed note misparses the Latin: `fortuna` is the *subject* — literally "(his)
  fortune will be good," not "he will be luck." Nobody embodies anything in this
  sentence grammatically. Your cultural instinct is still sound — Fortuna was a real
  goddess with major temples, and Romans did deify abstractions (Concordia, Fides,
  Victoria) — but this particular sentence is plain noun + adjective.

**Your questions, in order:**

- **`ius dicit` and the Supreme Court analogy** — the performative-speech insight is
  right and worth keeping (this is your third idiom correctly held: verba facit, odio
  habent, ius dicit). One refinement makes the analogy sharper: the praetor mostly did
  not decide cases. He presided over the *first* phase — deciding whether your claim was
  actionable, granting the formula, and publishing his edict of available remedies —
  then handed the case to a `iudex` (a private citizen) to try the facts. So the praetor
  is less "Justice deciding the case" and more "the authority who defines what counts as
  a legal claim at all." His annual edict became a rolling body of law
  (`ius honorarium` — law from the honores, literally the office-holders' law).

- **`rego, regere` for consuls — would a Roman say it?** Yes, comfortably, and your
  etymological instinct is exactly right: `rex`, `rego`, `rectus`, `regnum`, and
  `regula` all come from one root meaning "make straight, direct" (the same
  Indo-European root gives English *right* and *rule*, German *Reich*, Sanskrit
  *rajan*). The Roman allergy attached to the **noun and its derivatives** — rex,
  regnum, regnare, and the accusation `regnum appetere` ("grasping at kingship," flung
  at the Gracchi and Caesar) — not to the everyday verb. Cicero uses `regere` freely of
  legitimate governance and in De Re Publica calls his ideal statesman the
  `rector rei publicae`. So: `rem publicam regit` is unremarkable; `rem publicam regnat`
  would get you stabbed.

- **`nomina senatorum legit` → "he appoints the senators"** — your idiomatic conclusion
  is **correct**, and it's a real institutional catch: this is the `lectio senatus`,
  the censor's revision of the senate roll, the single most feared of his powers
  (being passed over = expulsion). But refine the *mechanism*: it isn't dico-style
  performative speech. `lego`'s root meaning is "gather, pick, **choose**" — "read" is
  the derived sense (picking letters off the page; same development in Greek λέγω).
  `legere senatum` = to *select* the senate. So the censor doesn't speak the roll into
  effect; he chooses it. Right answer, wrong tunnel — you routed it through the ius
  dicere insight, but the idiom lives in lego's semantics. (Bonus: `legio` — a
  "choosing" of soldiers — is the same word.)

- **`imperium` as Holy-Ghost-like indwelling power — you asked for a web check and
  challenges. Here's the honest scoreboard: your instinct is half right, and the half
  that's wrong is instructive.**

  *What holds up:* imperium did have a genuinely sacral dimension, and a real scholarly
  tradition (Mommsen onward; recently e.g. Berthelet on the
  [lex curiata and the religious nature of magistral power](https://www.academia.edu/1181961/_The_Curiate_Law_and_the_Religious_Nature_of_the_Power_of_Roman_Magistrates_))
  emphasizes it: conferral involved the [lex curiata de imperio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lex_curiata_de_imperio),
  the holder gained `auspicia maiora` (the right to consult Jupiter for the state), and
  the whole apparatus sat inside the pax deorum. Your urge to leave the word
  untranslated is also defensible — historians routinely do exactly that.

  *The challenges:* First, a strong modern line — [Drogula's *Commanders and Command*](https://bmcr.brynmawr.edu/2016/2016.06.28/)
  is the flagship — reads imperium prosaically: it is **military command authority**,
  legally defined, and carefully distinguished from `potestas` (civil magistral power)
  and `auspicium` (the religious channel). On this view the "mystical substance" reading
  is a modern romanticization; the divine-communication component Romans named
  *separately* — auspicium — and it traveled with imperium without being it. Second, and
  this is the sharper objection: indwelling-spirit language fits poorly with how
  imperium *behaves*. It was conferred by human legal act, bounded in space (a
  promagistrate's imperium **lapsed automatically** if he crossed the pomerium into the
  city — hence generals waiting outside Rome for their triumphs), bounded in time, and
  laid down on schedule — `imperium deponunt`, a phrase *you yourself translated
  correctly in w01-2*. A Christian would find "he set down the Holy Spirit at the end
  of his term, and it evaporated when he crossed the city limit" theologically
  incoherent; a Roman found the equivalent statement about imperium obvious. Possession
  language overstates it. A better analogy: a **military commission or judicial
  jurisdiction, solemnized religiously** — conferred, delimited, revocable, real. What
  your instinct correctly detects is that the Romans took the *conferral* seriously
  enough to route it through Jupiter; where it overshoots is making the power an
  inhabiting spirit rather than a bounded legal grant with divine sanction.
  (Accessible overview: [ACOUP, "How to Roman Republic 101: Imperium"](https://acoup.blog/2023/08/18/collections-how-to-roman-republic-101-part-iiib-imperium/).)

**Alternatives, not errors:** `cursum honorum` — when citing the name in English, use the
nominative *cursus honorum*; it's accusative in the Latin only because it's the object of
`vocant` (double accusative: "they call [it] [the cursus]"). `inter cives` — "among
citizens" slightly better than "between." `enim` — "for" (causal) over "indeed."
