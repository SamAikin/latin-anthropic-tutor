**The correction that matters: `Haec officia` → "This duty" — it's plural: "These duties
fathers leave to their sons."** `officia` is a neuter plural in `-a`, the same family as
`bella`, and this exact slip had just gone clean two assignments running — so the watch
reopens. In fairness, the trap here is double-layered: `haec` is **both** feminine
nominative singular (as in `Haec salutatio`, which you read correctly two paragraphs
earlier) **and** neuter nominative/accusative plural — identical surface form. You won't
get `hic`'s full paradigm until lesson w02-2; until then, let the noun cast the deciding
vote: `officium` is neuter, so `officia` is plural, whatever the pronoun seems to say.

**The sneakiest miss: `servant` → "serve."** `servo, servare` = *save, protect, keep
watch over* — it's on your Week 1 Anki list with exactly that gloss. "To serve" is a
different verb: `servio, servire`, and the giveaway is grammatical — `servio` takes the
**dative**, and `patronos` is accusative, so this can only be `servo`: "clients
faithfully look after their patrons." That's not a decorative difference; it's the point
of the sentence. The pair is built as a mirror — `viri nobiles clientes diligenter
curant, et clientes patronos fideliter servant` — care running down, protection running
up. This joins the false-friend list (#6), and it's the most dangerous member yet:
"clients serve patrons" is so contextually plausible that your odd-English alarm never
had a chance to ring. Only the morphology could have saved you — which is the lesson.
English inheritance splits the pair helpfully: *preserve/conserve/observe* descend from
`servo`; *serve/servant* descend from `servus` (the slave) and `servio`.

**`salutant` / `salutatio` → "salute": second lean on a corrected word.** We flagged
`salutabat` → "saluted" in w01-4: `salutare` = *to greet, pay one's respects*; the
`salutatio` is the morning greeting-call, and there's no military gesture anywhere in
it. You clearly understood the scene — but your corrections normally stick on the first
telling, and this one didn't, so it gets a better hook this time: `salutare` is
literally "to wish *salus*" — health, safety (that's the root of `salve!`, the everyday
Roman hello, and `salus` itself is coming on your Week 4 list). A Roman greeting is a
little health-wish. If it slips a third time we'll drill it properly.

**A near-invisible one: `consilium` → "council."** You wrote "gives them council in

**Your adverb discovery — real morphology, correctly researched, and your two wild
specimens happen to be the two subtypes of the rule.** The terminology you asked for:
these are **deadjectival adverbs of manner**, and traditional grammars call the classes
"adverbs in *-ē*" (1st/2nd-declension adjectives) and "adverbs in *-iter*"
(3rd-declension). The process itself is just derivation — you'll sometimes see
"adverbialization." One refinement and then the traps you asked to be primed for:

*The refinement:* build from the **stem** (your genitive rule again). `fidelis` →
`fidel-` + `-iter` → `fideliter`, exactly as you said. But adjectives whose stem ends in
`-nt-` (the `diligens, diligentis` type) take bare `-er`, not `-iter`: `diligent-` +
`-er` → `diligenter`, likewise `prudenter`, `sapienter`. So the reading handed you one
of each: `fideliter` (regular `-iter`) and `diligenter` (the `-nt-` subtype).

*The traps:*
1. **`facile`** — `facilis` does *not* make ✗`faciliter` in classical prose. The adverb
   is `facile` — the neuter accusative singular used adverbially. This is a whole
   family: `multum`, `primum`, `paulum`, `ceterum` are all neuter-accusative adverbs.
   You've already met one in the wild: `facile vident` in reading w01-4.
2. **`magne` effectively doesn't exist.** Derivation says `magne`; usage says
   `magnopere` ("greatly") or `valde`, with `maxime` as the superlative. Same lesson as
   your principal-parts finding last homework: the highest-frequency words are exactly
   where derive-by-rule fails and recall has to take over.
3. **`bene` and `male`** — from `bonus` and `malus`, but with irregular short *-e* (not
   ✗`bonē`). Two of the most common adverbs in the language, both slightly lawless.
4. **A clutch of 1st/2nd adjectives prefer `-ō`:** `subitō`, `rārō`, `prīmō`, `falsō` —
   ablative-shaped adverbs living alongside the `-ē` type.
5. **Comparison collapses the two classes.** Comparative adverb = neuter accusative of
   the comparative adjective (`fortius` = "more bravely"); superlative in `-issimē`
   (`fortissimē`). So the full ladder for your specimen: `fideliter`, `fidelius`,
   `fidelissime` — the `-iter` appears only in the positive.
