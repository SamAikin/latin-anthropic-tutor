## Evaluation

**Overall: comprehended, and this was the trickiest tense environment yet — you passed
it.** The text deliberately mixes three layers: perfect narrative spine (`fuit`,
`venerunt`, `tulit`, `audiverunt`), imperfect background (`gerebant`, `salutabat`,
`ibant`, `gaudebant`), and *gnomic presents* in the explanatory asides (`creat`,
`vident`, `ferunt`, `habet`, `creat` again) — the "this is how Romans do things in
general" voice cutting into a story about yesterday. You tracked every switch. Nobody
flattened anything. And the pluperfect: you're right that spotting `creaverat` → "had
elected" on your own "isn't nothing" — it's a tense you haven't been taught (w01-3 is
next), and you caught *why* it's pluperfect: by the time everyone heard the names, the
electing was already complete. That's aspect reasoning, not just ending-matching.

**A quieter win worth naming:** `nomina nova` → "the new names" and `suffragia` →
"votes." Neuter plurals in `-a` have been sliding to singular in your work three times
running (`bella` → "war" twice). This reading is the first clean pass — every neuter
plural came out plural.

**The one correction that matters: `nuntii` are messengers, not news.** `Nuntius` is
polysemous — the man who carries the message, or the message itself. Here the grammar
decides it: `nuntii ... ierunt et clamaverunt` — the nuntii *went* through the streets
and *shouted*. News doesn't walk and yell; people do. "Messengers went through the
streets of the city and shouted." Notice what your own English did: "the news traveled
... and **they** cried out" — that dangling "they" with no antecedent is exactly the
smell we flagged in w01-2 (consilium): **when your English output comes out semantically
odd, suspect a polysemous Latin word.** The alarm was ringing; next time let it stop
you.

**Smaller corrections:**
- **`togas candidas` → "the candidate toga"** — `candidus` means *gleaming white*
  (candeo, to shine). The etymology runs the other way: the toga isn't named for the
  candidate; the candidate is named for the toga. "They wore whitened togas" —
  chalk-brightened, so you could spot an office-seeker across the Forum. (Also plural:
  each candidate his own toga.)
- **`ita enim cives candidatos facile vident`** — "so that" reads it as a purpose
  clause, but `enim` is the explanation particle: "**for in this way** the citizens
  easily see the candidates." Same comprehension, different structure — the sentence
  explains why the togas work, it doesn't state the wearers' purpose. When you see
  `enim`, expect "for..."
- **`inter homines` → "between men"** — with a plural crowd, `inter` is "among": the
  candidates' friends moved *among the people*, working the crowd. (Same note as
  `inter cives` last time — "between" wants exactly two.)
- **`magnam auctoritatem` → "the greatest influence"** — `magnam` is just "great";
  "greatest" would be `maximam`. I suspect your history sense inflated it — and
  historically you're not wrong (see below) — but keep the degree the Latin gives you.
- **`salutabat` → "saluted"** — `salutare` is simply "to greet" (the morning
  *salutatio* is the greeting-call, not a military gesture). Cognate pulling ahead of
  meaning again, mildly.
- **On all the "used to X" imperfects** — you identified every imperfect correctly, so
  this is refinement, not correction: English "used to" is the *habitual* reading, but
  inside a story framed by `Heri` ("yesterday was a great day") the imperfects are
  *descriptive* — "the candidates **were wearing** white togas, each one **was
  greeting** his friends." You actually did this instinctively in the last paragraph
  ("men **were rejoicing**"). The imperfect has both faces; let the frame pick. This is
  exactly w01-3 territory, so consider it a preview.

**Your handshake note — the instinct is exactly right, and it's a real Roman thing.**
`Manus tenebat` literally "held hands," and the technical term for a candidate's
glad-handing was `prensare` — "to grab repeatedly." Cicero, reporting the consular field
for 63, writes `prensat unus P. Galba` — "only Galba is out pressing hands (already)"
(Att. 1.1). There was a whole how-to-campaign manual attributed to his brother Quintus
(the *Commentariolum Petitionis*) that advises exactly this: learn names, grip hands,
promise freely — your sentence `promittebat multa` is straight out of its cynical heart.

**Your question — why elections outside the walls — is a better catch than you may
realize.** The assembly in this reading is the *comitia centuriata*, the one that
elects consuls and praetors, and it is organized by *centuries* — military units. In
origin it simply **is the Roman army, mustered and voting**. And the army may not
assemble inside the *pomerium*: Roman constitutional space is split into `domi` (at
home — civil authority, citizens) and `militiae` (on campaign — military authority,
soldiers), and the pomerium is the line between them. So the centuriate assembly meets
on the Campus Martius — the *field of Mars*, the mustering and drill ground — because
a Roman voting in his century is, in legal fiction, a soldier standing in formation.
Three refinements to your Rubicon analogy:

1. **You've actually met this boundary before** — it's the same pomerium rule from your
   imperium discussion: a promagistrate's military imperium lapsed the moment he
   crossed the pomerium, which is why generals awaiting triumphs camped outside the
   city. One line, both directions: military power doesn't come in; the army-as-assembly
   doesn't either.
2. **The Rubicon is the same principle at a different line.** It marked the boundary of
   Cisalpine Gaul, Caesar's *provincia* — a general leading troops beyond his assigned
   province was in armed insurrection. So: pomerium = the city-scale line, provincial
   boundaries = the empire-scale lines, same constitutional idea — armed force is
   quarantined away from civic space.
3. **Only this assembly had to meet outside.** The *comitia tributa* and the plebeian
   council — organized by tribes, civilian in character — met in the Forum or on the
   Capitol, inside. The military framing is specific to the centuriata.

One last flourish you'll enjoy: during a centuriate assembly a red flag flew on the
Janiculum hill — in the old days its lowering meant enemies approaching, drop your
ballots and man the walls. The rule outlived the threat, and in **63 BC** (Cicero's
consulship — week 4 territory) the praetor Metellus Celer killed a politically charged
trial before the centuriate assembly by having that flag hauled down, instantly
dissolving the meeting. The letter of a military rule, deployed as pure procedural
sabotage.

**And your `pars prima`:** that's the *centuria praerogativa* — one century of the
first class, chosen by lot, voting first, its result announced before anyone else
voted. Cicero says its verdict carried such weight as an *omen* that the candidate it
named almost always won (Pro Murena 38). So `magnam auctoritatem` is, if anything, the
reading being restrained.

**On the ablative of means (`non ferro, sed suffragiis`):** consulting the grammar
reference was exactly the right move — that construction is the literal subject of your
next lesson (w01-4: prepositions and case idioms), so you met it a lesson early and
armed yourself. Note also the metonymy you sailed through without comment: `ferrum` is
"iron" standing for the sword. "Not by iron, but by ballots" — that closing line is the
whole republican ideal in five words, and you rendered it perfectly.
