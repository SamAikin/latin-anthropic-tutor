# Daily Reading — w03-4

## Provenance and Citations

**ADAPTED HISTORICAL TEXT.** Source: Cornelius Nepos, *Life of Atticus* 1, verified
against the Perseus Digital Library text (perseus.tufts.edu, retrieved 2026-07-11).
Nepos is the real thing for this course: a genuine late-republic author (c. 110–24 BC),
writing about his own friend. Titus Pomponius Atticus — Cicero's closest friend and
lifelong correspondent — was an equestrian who navigated every civil war of the age
without joining any side. This chapter covers his boyhood.

Changes from the original, made to match your current level: two result clauses ("ut
non solum... acciperet", "ut nemo iis... fuerit carior" — subjunctive, not yet taught)
recast as indicative statements; "prout ipse amabat litteras" simplified to a relative
clause; "ferebatur clariusque exsplendescebat" simplified to "clarus erat". The perfect
participles "generatus" and "acceptam" are Nepos's own and are deliberately retained —
they are this week's grammar. The deponent "usus est" (patre usus est = "he had a
father", literally "he enjoyed a father") is also Nepos's own.

## Reading

T. Pomponius Atticus, ab origine ultima stirpis Romanae generatus, dignitatem equestrem
a maioribus acceptam perpetuo obtinuit. Patre usus est diligente et — ut tum erant
tempora — diti, qui litteras inprimis amabat. Pater filium omnibus doctrinis, quas
puerilis aetas accipere debet, erudivit.

Erat autem in puero praeter docilitatem ingenii summa suavitas oris atque vocis:
celeriter accipiebat quae tradebantur, et excellenter pronuntiabat. Qua ex re in
pueritia inter aequales clarus erat — clarior quam generosi condiscipuli animo aequo
ferre poterant. Itaque omnes studio suo incitabat.

In hoc numero fuerunt L. Torquatus, C. Marius filius, M. Cicero: quos amicitia sua sic
devinxit ut nemo eis per totam vitam carior fuit.
