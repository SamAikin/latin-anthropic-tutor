# Daily Reading — w04-1

## Provenance and Citations

**ADAPTED HISTORICAL TEXT.** Source: Cornelius Nepos, *Life of Atticus* 2, verified
against the Perseus Digital Library text (perseus.tufts.edu, retrieved 2026-07-11). The
events: after Sulpicius's murder (the same Sulpicius from reading w02-4), the young
Atticus reads the political weather of the Cinnan years and moves himself — and his
money — to Athens, while still quietly helping the younger Marius, a declared public
enemy. You met these events from the outside in week 2; here they are again from inside
one careful man's life.

Changes from the original: "quin alterutram partem offenderet" and the result clause
about the Athenians (subjunctive, not yet taught) are recast as indicative; "ratus" is
expanded to "putavit"; the passage on his interest-free public loans is compressed. The
indirect statement "vidit ... civitatem esse perturbatam" is Nepos's own and is
deliberately retained — it is this week's grammar, in the wild. So is the participle
phrase "hostem iudicatum" (week 3's grammar).

## Reading

Pater Attici mature decessit. Ipse adulescentulus, propter affinitatem P. Sulpicii, qui
tribunus plebis interfectus est, non expers fuit illius periculi: namque Anicia,
consobrina Pomponii, Servio, fratri Sulpicii, nupserat.

Itaque, interfecto Sulpicio, Atticus vidit Cinnano tumultu civitatem esse perturbatam;
vidit etiam sibi facultatem pro dignitate vivendi non dari, nam animi civium dissociati
erant: alii Sullanis, alii Cinnanis partibus favebant. Tempus igitur idoneum studiis
suis esse putavit et Athenas se contulit. Tamen adulescentem Marium, hostem iudicatum,
opibus suis iuvit et fugam eius pecunia sublevavit. Et ne fortuna sua periret, magnam
partem fortunarum suarum eodem Athenas traiecit.

Athenis ita vixit: universis Atheniensibus merito carissimus erat. Nam saepe suis opibus
inopiam publicam civitatis levavit; cum civitas pecuniam petere debebat, ipse semper
dabat, neque usuram umquam accepit. Praeterea universos cives frumento donavit.
