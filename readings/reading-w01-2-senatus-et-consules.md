# Daily Reading — w01-2

## Provenance and Citations

**GENERATED.** Composed entirely by the tutor model for this course (2026-07-11); not a
historical text. Historical content: a simplified but accurate sketch of the senate and
the consulship. The expulsion of Tarquinius Superbus (traditionally 509 BC) is told here
as the Romans themselves told it — it is founding legend as much as history, and Roman
writers treated it as the origin story of the republic.

## Reading

Senatus est consilium publicum Romanorum. Senatores in curia conveniunt et de bello, de
pace, de legibus sententias dicunt. Senatus leges non facit — leges populus in comitiis
facit — sed auctoritas senatus magna est, et magistratus consilium senatus raro
relinquunt.

Olim reges urbem regebant. Sed rex ultimus, Tarquinius Superbus, vir malus et crudelis
erat. Itaque cives regem expulerunt et rem publicam fecerunt. Ex illo tempore Romani
regnum timent et nomen regis odio habent.

Nunc populus quotannis duos consules creat. Consules imperium tenent: exercitus ducunt,
senatum vocant, leges ad populum ferunt. Sed imperium eorum fines habet. Post unum annum
consules imperium deponunt, et si unus consul errat, alter eum prohibere potest. Sic
Romani unum dominum non habent: leges, non homines, civitatem regunt.

[Note: added to Anki: `sententia`, `comitium`, `auctoritas`]

## Translation

The Senate is the public plan of the romans. Senators meet in the curia, and discuss opinions about war, about peace, about laws.
The Senate does not make laws - the people make laws in the assemblies - but the authority of the senate is great, and magistrates
seldom abandon the plans of the Senate.

Once kings ruled the city. But the final king, Tarquinius Superbus, was an evil and cruel man. And therefore the citizens expelled the king,
and made the Republic. Since then (lit. from that time) the Romans fear royal power and hate the name of the king.

Now the people annually elect two consuls. Consuls hold imperium: they lead the armies [note: unclear if sg or pl but each roman legion is often
talked about as "an army" and when consuls or proconsuls were given multiple legions like during the rebellion of Spartacus we generally say things
like "the consular armies" - let's not be nit picky when contrasting historic and current military terms and structures as they are different
and the broad concepts are what matter.], they summon the Senate, they bring laws to the people. But their imperium has boundaries. After one
year consuls set aside the imperium, and if one consul errs, the other can restrain him. Thus the romans do not have one master: laws,
not men, rule the state.

## Evaluation

**Overall: genuine in-situ comprehension, and the cleanest tense discipline you've shown
yet.** The narrative tracked start to finish, the time layers are all correctly rendered —
imperfect `regebant` ("ruled," ongoing) against perfect `expulerunt`/`fecerunt` (one-time
events), presents kept present throughout — and `relinquunt` came out correctly present
after being read as past in Monday's homework. That's the exact slip, fixed, on the same
verb. Also notable: `eorum`, `eum`, and `alter` all handled correctly *before* the pronoun
lesson (that's w02-2 material), and `regnum` → "royal power" is precisely the right sense
here (kingship-as-institution, not territory). `odio habent` → "hate" nails an idiom
(literally "hold in hatred").

**The one real miss — first sentence:** `consilium publicum` → "the public plan." Here
`consilium` is a **third** sense: "council, deliberative body" — "the Senate is the public
council of the Romans." You were corrected from "consensus" to "plan" in w01-1 and that
stuck, but the word has a range: **plan / advice, counsel / judgment / council**. (Your
own vocab.md entry lists "council.") The tell was available in the English: "the Senate is
the public plan of the Romans" doesn't quite mean anything — when your output sentence
comes out semantically odd, suspect that a polysemous word wants a different sense.
To be clear, "plan" is not wrong as a default; it just isn't the only card in the deck.
Same word in sentence 3: `consilium senatus raro relinquunt` — "advice" fits better than
"plans" ("magistrates rarely disregard the senate's advice"), though "plans" is defensible
there.

**Your bracketed `exercitus` question — good catch, and the ambiguity is real but
morphological, not just semantic.** `exercitus` is 4th declension (exercitus, -ūs), which
you haven't had yet (it's lesson w02-1). Its accusative plural is `exercitūs` — identical
in spelling to the nominative singular once macrons are stripped, which they are in
readings by our style contract. So the form itself is genuinely ambiguous on the page;
"armies" (plural) is the natural read with two consuls as subject, and that's what I
intended. Your instinct to flag it rather than silently pick was exactly right. No
nit-picking on the military-terminology point — agreed, and "armies" is what the Latin
says.

**Alternatives and color, not errors:**
- `sententias dicunt` → "discuss opinions" — comprehended fine. For the historical file:
  `sententiam dicere` is the technical term for a senator formally delivering his opinion
  when called on in rank order. Your Anki gloss ("vote in the senate") covers this.
- `leges ad populum ferunt` → "bring laws to the people" — literal and correct. `legem
  ferre` is the technical idiom "to propose/carry a law" before an assembly; a passed law
  is `lex lata`, from the same verb — which is where English **legislator** comes from
  (lex + lator, "law-bearer").
- "the plans of the Senate" for singular `consilium` — harmless number smoothing in
  English, not a parse error.

**One Anki flag:** you added `comitium` — note that in the "assembly" sense the word is
**plural-only: comitia, comitiorum (n. pl.)**. Singular `comitium` means the physical
space in the Forum where assemblies met. Also, `comitia` is already on your assigned
Week 1 vocab list, so check you haven't just made a near-duplicate card; if you keep
both, keep the sg./pl. distinction on them.
