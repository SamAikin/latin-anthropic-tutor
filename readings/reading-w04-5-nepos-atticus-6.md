# Daily Reading — w04-5

## Provenance and Citations

**ADAPTED HISTORICAL TEXT (heavily).** Source: Cornelius Nepos, *Life of Atticus* 6,
verified against the Perseus Digital Library text (perseus.tufts.edu, retrieved
2026-07-11). The subject: how Atticus lived through the collapse — sympathetic to the
optimates but refusing offices, prosecutions, and the profits of other men's ruin. The
historical context for one line: after the proscriptions (reading w02-5), the property
of the condemned was sold at public auction; fortunes were made there by men who asked
no questions. Atticus never went.

Changes from the original: this chapter is dense with subjunctive constructions (result
clauses, cum-clauses, subordinate clauses in reported thought — block 2 grammar), so it
has been substantially recast into indicative sentences and accusative-plus-infinitive
indirect statements. Nepos's central metaphor — the man who commits himself to civil
storms is no more his own master than the man tossed by storms at sea — and his blunt
catalogue of the things Atticus never did are preserved with his own vocabulary. Read
the original again in a later block; it will be worth it twice.

## Reading

In re publica Atticus ita se gessit: semper optimarum partium et erat et existimabatur,
neque tamen se civilibus fluctibus committebat. Existimabat enim eos, qui se his
fluctibus dederant, non magis in sua potestate esse quam eos qui maritimis fluctibus
iactabantur.

Honores non petivit, quamquam ei propter gratiam dignitatemque patebant; nam illis
temporibus honores neque more maiorum peti neque, conservatis legibus, capi poterant.
Ad hastam publicam numquam accessit; sciebat enim illam praedam ex sanguine civium
venire.

Neminem umquam accusavit, neque suo nomine neque subscribens. In ius de sua re numquam
iit; iudicium nullum habuit. Multi eum ideo timidum vocabant; sed omnes intellegebant
eum haec officio, non timore neque spe, facere. Itaque observantia eius omnibus carior
erat quam officia eorum qui potentibus serviebant.
