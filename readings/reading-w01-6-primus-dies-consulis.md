## Reading

Primus dies mensis Ianuarii erat. Gaius, vir nobilis, consulatum diu petiverat, et heri
anno novo imperium acceperat: populus enim eum mense Novembri consulem creaverat. Hodie
primum diem consulatus sui aget.

Ante lucem Gaius surrexit. Amici et clientes iam venerant et totam domum impleverant;
omnes novum consulem salutabant. Tum Gaius cum senatoribus et amicis ad Capitolium iit,
nam novi consules primo die Iovi sacra faciunt. Populus in viis stabat et magna voce
clamabat.

Post sacra Gaius senatum in curiam vocavit et verba fecit: "Patres conscripti, magna
officia nos exspectant. Bella, leges, pecunia publica — de omnibus rebus consilium
vestrum petam. Si officium meum bene gessero, post annum imperium laetus deponam; nam
consul bonus rem publicam servat, non se ipsum."

Senatores sententias suas dixerunt, et novus consul diligenter audiebat. Sic primus
dies consulatus fuit: non proelium, sed officium.

## Translation

It was the first day of the month of January. Gaius, a noble man, had campagined for a long time
for the consulship, and yesterday at the new year he had recieved imperium: for the
people had elected him consul in the month of November. Today he is experiencing the first
day of his consulship. [note: had to do a little research as the difference between `suus` and `eius`
makes sense but isn't totally intuitive since we don't make that distinction in english.]

Before dawn Gaius arose. Friends and retainers had come already, nd they had filled the whole
house; everyone greeted the new consul. [note: using the simple past here even though verb is imperfect]
Then Gaius walked with the senators and friends to the the Capitolium [note: Jupiter's temple],
for new consuls on the first day conduct the sacred rights for Jupiter. [note: intentionally loose
translation of `facio` because in english we speak more about "receiving" or "conducting" sacred rights].
The people stood in the streets and cheered with a great voice [note: abl of means here I assume?].

After the rights Gaius called the Senate into the Curia and said: "Enrolled fathers [note: the formal and 
slightly pedantic way to say "senators" in rome I'm gathering] great duties await us. Wars, laws, the
public funds - I will seek your counsel on all things. If I will have conducted my duty well, after
a year I will happily lay down the imperium [note: not sure if "the imperium" or "imperium" is a 
more standard translation her]; for a good consul protects the republic, not he himself."

The senators spoke their opinions, and the new counsul listened carefully. Thus was the first
day of the consulship: not combat, but duty.

## Evaluation

**Overall: comprehended, and this is the cleanest reading of the week — fitting, since
it was built as the week-1 synthesis piece.** The design brief was the full tense
system in one narrative: pluperfect backstory, perfect narrative spine, imperfect
background, gnomic-present asides, and a future + future perfect payload in the speech.
You tracked essentially all of it. The pluperfects (`petiverat`, `acceperat`,
`creaverat`, `venerant`, `impleverant`) came out "had X-ed" every time; the gnomic
aside (`novi consules... sacra faciunt`) stayed present inside a past-tense narrative
without wobbling; and — the sentence the whole reading was aimed at — `Si officium
meum bene gessero... deponam` came out "If I *will have conducted* my duty well... I
will lay down." That stiff English is exactly the evidence I want: it proves the
future perfect was *seen*, same as your si-clause in w01-3. One tense miss in the
whole piece, covered below.

**The watch-list results, because several long-running items just resolved at once:**

- **`magna officia nos exspectant` → "great duties await us" — a triple pass.**
  `officia` plural (the *exact word* that reopened the neuter-plural watch in w01-5),
  `exspectant` → "await" (false friend #2, once "expect"), and `magna` → "great," not
  "greatest." Then two words later, `Bella` → "Wars" — the word that started the
  neuter-plural watch and failed twice. Both signature words clean in one reading:
  **the neuter-plural watch closes again**, and this time on its own home turf.
- **`servat` → "protects."** One reading after `servant` → "serve" (false friend #6,
  the dangerous one), the correction held: "a good consul protects the republic."
  Which is also the sentence's actual point — `servare` vs. serving oneself is the
  whole moral of Gaius's speech.
- **`salutabant` → "greeted."** After two "salute" slips, the salus-hook did its job.
  No drill needed.
- **`enim` → "for."** First time. It was "so that" in w01-4 and "truly" in w01-5;
  "for the people had elected him" is the real signpost reading. Watch closed.
- **`consulatum diu petiverat` → "had campaigned for the consulship."** This is the
  best vocabulary moment in the translation. `peto` was a near-miss word two weeks
  running ("achieve"), and the fix was the idiom `honores petere` = to stand for
  office. You didn't just avoid the miss — you deployed the idiom: "campaigned for"
  is precisely what `consulatum petere` means. Likewise `acceperat` → "received"
  (once "seize") and `consilium vestrum petam` → "I will seek your counsel" — right
  sense of `peto`, right sense of `consilium`, and spelled *counsel* this time.
- **`iit` → past.** The perfect of `eo` flattened to imperfect in the w01-3 homework
  (`ierat`); here the bare `iit` — the hardest form of the family to spot — read as a
  completed past. ("Went" rather than "walked," but that's smoothing, not parsing.)

**The one real miss: `Hodie primum diem consulatus sui aget` → "Today he is
experiencing..." — `aget` is future: "Today he *will spend* the first day of his
consulship."** This is the exact trap from lesson w01-3: `ago` is 3rd conjugation, so
`-et` is future, not present (the `ponet` lesson). The reflex fired in the homework
(`petent`, `creabit` both correct) but not here — and I can see why: `Hodie` pulls
English hard toward the present, and the narrative hasn't started yet so nothing
contradicts it. Note what stayed intact, though: in the same reading you read `petam`
and `deponam` — 1st-singular futures — correctly. So this isn't the future tense
wobbling; it's specifically the slippery 3rd-singular `-et` slot when context doesn't
force the issue. The watch reopens, narrowly: **when you hit `-et`/`-it`, check the
conjugation before the context.** (Your "experiencing" for `diem agere` is otherwise
fine — "to spend/pass the day" is the idiom, so the sense was right; only the time
reference slipped.)

**Answers to your bracketed notes — all of which were good questions:**

- **`suus` vs. `eius`: your research got it right.** `suus` is *reflexive* — it points
  back to the subject of its clause. `consulatus sui` = "of his *own* consulship"
  (Gaius's, because Gaius is the subject). `eius` would point at somebody *else*:
  `patrem suum amat` = he loves his own father; `patrem eius amat` = he loves that
  other man's father. English collapses both into "his," which is why it feels
  unintuitive — Latin never has the ambiguity. Full pronoun treatment is lesson
  w02-2; you've now primed it, which has become a habit of yours.
- **Imperfect `salutabant` → simple past, flagged:** exactly the right call, and the
  flag is what makes it right. "Everyone *was greeting* / *kept greeting*" is the
  fuller background-action rendering, but English narrative tolerates the simple past
  here, and your note proves the aspect was seen. That's the deal: smooth the English
  however you like, as long as you show me you saw the Latin.
- **`magna voce` — you asked "abl of means?": close — this one is usually classed as
  ablative of *manner*.** The boundary: means is the *instrument* ("with a sword,"
  `ferro`), manner is the *how* ("with great enthusiasm"). Manner ordinarily wants
  `cum`, but when an adjective is present, `cum` becomes optional — `magna (cum)
  voce`. It's a genuinely fuzzy border (a voice is arguably an instrument of
  shouting), and grammarians have argued about cases like this one; your instinct to
  reach for an ablative-flavor label at all is the skill. Lesson w01-4's territory,
  and you keep showing up to lessons early.
- **`sacra faciunt` → "conduct the sacred rites":** your "intentionally loose" note
  undersells it — that's not loose, that's correct. `sacra facere` is the standard
  idiom for performing sacrifice/ritual; nobody translates it "make sacred things."
  One spelling note in passing: the English word is **rites** (ritual, from Latin
  `ritus`), not "rights" (`iura`) — a pure homophone slip, but in a Roman context the
  difference between rites and rights is worth keeping crisp.
- **`Patres conscripti` → your "formal and slightly pedantic" gathering is exactly
  right.** It's the fixed formal address for the assembled senate. The traditional
  story: the original patrician members were the `patres`, and when new members were
  enrolled to fill the ranks (tradition says after the kings were expelled), they were
  the `conscripti` — "the enrolled." `patres et conscripti` fused into one phrase.
  Cicero opens speeches with it constantly; you'll meet it again in week 4 with
  *In Catilinam*.
- **"the imperium" vs. "imperium":** either is standard. Scholarly English usually
  treats `imperium` as a technical loanword and runs it without an article or with
  "his" ("laid down his imperium"); "the imperium" is fine too. Given how much
  mileage you've gotten out of the imperium thread — and note that `imperium laetus
  deponam` is your own imperium-deponere observation from w01-3, written into Gaius's
  mouth — I'd keep it as the Latin word. Small polish on the same clause: `laetus` is
  an adjective ("glad") used predicatively, and your "happily" is the idiomatic way
  to English it — Latin prefers the adjective where English prefers the adverb. Well
  handled, probably without noticing why.

**Two small notes, neither a comprehension problem:**

- `verba fecit` → "said" — comprehended (third meeting with this idiom), but it
  flattens a formal register: `verba facere` is to *make a formal address*, the
  parliamentary "to have the floor." "Addressed them" keeps the ceremony of the
  moment audible.
- `non se ipsum` → "not he himself" — the Latin is accusative (object of `servat`),
  so English wants "not *himself*": "a good consul protects the republic, not
  himself." Your rendering suggests the contrast landed but the English case slipped.
  `se`/`ipse` get their full treatment in w02-2.

**A closing historical reward, since `sententias suas dixerunt` deserves it:**
`sententiam dicere` is the technical term of senate procedure — when the presiding
consul put a question (`referre ad senatum`), each senator was asked his opinion *in
rank order* (consuls-elect first, then ex-consuls, on down), and delivering it was
"speaking one's sententia." Some senators expressed an opinion merely by walking to
sit near the man they agreed with — voting with their feet, literally
(`pedibus in sententiam ire`, whence our "pedarian" backbenchers). `sententia` is on
your own Anki list by your own hand — you added it after reading w01-2 — so the word
has now gone from self-add to read-in-context. That's the loop working.

for week 2.
