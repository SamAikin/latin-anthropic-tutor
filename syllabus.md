Generated 2026-07-11 (first syllabus — First-Run Bootstrap). Covers Block 1: Weeks 1–4,

## Historical Context

Nothing precedes this block. The framework was designed and reviewed 2026-07-11; a
placement diagnostic the same day found: declensions 1–3, the full active indicative,
the perfect-system passive, relative clauses, and ut-purpose clauses all still active
from school Latin. The rust line sits at the participle system (attributive participles,
ablative absolute), indirect statement (accusative + infinitive), and conditions.
Vocabulary — especially high-frequency verbs — is the weakest layer. Grammar
metalanguage is strong: the learner can *name* constructions he cannot yet *read*.
Block 1 is built directly against that map.

## Current 4 Week Plan

The arc: weeks 1–2 re-consolidate the foundations at speed (rusty, not absent); weeks
3–4 attack the rust line itself. Vocab is frequency-weighted throughout, with principal
parts emphasized from day one — the participle system (week 3) is built out of them.

## North Star

Deep reading fluency in the Latin of the late republic — acquiring the language and
studying the late republic **through** the language. Cicero is the guiding inspiration;
Caesar and others are read as history demands, but no single viewpoint should
predominate. Every 4-week block should move us toward reading real late-republic texts
with real ergonomics (no scaffolding, no modern conveniences).

The late republic is the target *dialect*, not a fence: earlier-republic and
late-empire authors are welcome from time to time as lessons lend themselves, for a
well-rounded experience of the language. Cicero is the elder statesman target, but most
readings will not be his — the aim is the full vista of the Roman experiment: its most
enlightened and democratic inventions, and its darkest lessons on the banality of
systemic evil, read as the cautionary tale it is.
