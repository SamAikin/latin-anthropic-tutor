# Lesson-w01-4

## Primer

Week 1 closes with the connective tissue: prepositions and the case idioms that don't
need prepositions at all. You handled `in senatu` slightly loosely in the diagnostic
("telling the senate" for "speaking in the senate") — today makes that distinction
mechanical. The two systems to internalize: (1) prepositions govern a case, and for
in/sub the case changes the meaning (motion vs. location); (2) Latin marks time, means,
and manner with bare cases where English needs a preposition.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept: preposition + case = meaning; bare case = idiom.

1. **Hook (4 min).** `Consul in senatu dicebat` vs. `Consul in senatum venit.` Same
   preposition, different case, different picture. (This is his diagnostic sentence,
   corrected — name that openly; closing a known gap feels good.)
2. **Preposition families (10 min).** With accusative (motion/extent): ad, in, per,
   trans, inter, ob, propter, ante, post, contra. With ablative (location/source/
   accompaniment): in, ab, ex, de, cum, sine, pro. Socratic sort: give him a mixed
   list, he sorts by case and justifies. Flag ab/ex/de nuance lightly (away from /
   out of / down from, all "from"-family).
3. **Bare-case idioms (12 min).** Time when/within = ablative (illo die, tribus
   annis); time duration = accusative (multos annos); means = ablative, no preposition
   (ferro et igni); manner = cum + abl or bare abl with adjective (magna voce);
   agent = a/ab + ablative ONLY with passives — connect to `a militibus capta est`,
   which he read perfectly in the diagnostic. Drill: translate 6 short phrases both
   directions.
4. **Wrap (4 min).** Homework (~30 min). Week 1 done: nouns, adjectives, verbs,
   prepositions — the whole indicative sentence machine. Week 2 completes the noun
   system (decl 4–5) and opens the passive.
