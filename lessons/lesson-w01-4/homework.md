### A — corrections

**A1. `fluminem` → `flūmen`.** This is the only real error on the page.
`flūmen, flūminis` is 3rd declension **neuter**, and the iron rule for every neuter noun
in Latin is: **nominative and accusative are always identical, in both numbers.** So
acc. sg. is `flūmen`, unchanged. Your reasoning was sound and mechanical — find the stem
(`flūmin-`), add the 3rd-decl. acc. sg. ending (`-em`) — and that procedure is exactly
right for masculines and feminines (`mīles → mīlitem`, `urbs → urbem`). Neuters opt out
of it. The tell is that the neuter plural gives it away too: `flūmina` for both nom. and
acc. Worth a deliberate pass, because this same rule governs `nōmen`, `corpus`, `tempus`,
`iter` — high-frequency nouns you will meet constantly.

**A6. `Illa diē` → `Illō diē`.** Half right, and the half you got right is the harder half.
- `diē` is **correct** — `diēs, diēī` is 5th declension, abl. sg. `-ē`. You have not been
  taught 5th declension (it's Week 2). Getting the noun form right on an untaught
  declension is a genuine win.
- `illā` should be `illō`, because **`diēs` is masculine.** It's an oddity worth
  memorizing: `diēs` is masculine in general use, and feminine only when it means a
  *specific appointed day* — a deadline, a due date (`dīem cōnstituit`, but `certā diē`).
  You defaulted to feminine, reasonably, since `-ēs` looks feminine and most 5th-decl.
  nouns (`rēs`, `spēs`, `fidēs`) are. `diēs` is the exception.
- Structurally: your case choice was **right** — time when = ablative. That was the
  question being asked, and you answered it.

**Correct and worth noting:**
- **A5 `senātū`** — `senātus, -ūs` is 4th declension, abl. sg. `-ū`. Also untaught. If you
  reasoned it as 2nd declension you'd have written `senātō`; you didn't.
- **A3 `armīs`** — `arma, -ōrum` is a *plurale tantum* (no singular exists), so "abl. pl."
  is the only option. Your parenthetical shows you saw that.
- **A2** — your note about the singular is right; `dē bellō` is what the sentence wants.

### B — 8/8

**B3 `paucīs diēbus` labelled "time within."** This was on my watch-list from the lesson:
you translated `duābus hōrīs` correctly but mislabelled it "time when." **The label stuck.**
Your bracketed note ruling out dative is also correct reasoning — `-ibus` is genuinely
ambiguous in form, and you resolved it by construction rather than by ending, which is
the only way it can be resolved.

**B8 `prō patriā` — full credit, and the item is defective, not you.** The question offered
six labels and `prō patriā` is none of them. It's *benefactive* — "on behalf of." Your
"abl. of motivation, for lack of a better word" is the right instinct plus an accurate
flag that the box doesn't fit. For the record, `prō`'s core sense is spatial: **"in front
of"** (`prō castrīs`, drawn up in front of the camp). Standing in front of something →
defending it → acting on its behalf. `Prō patriā morī` is literally "to die in front of
the fatherland," and the metaphor was never fully dead to a Roman ear.

**B5 `magnā vōce`** — "with a great voice." Idiomatically *in a loud voice*, and yes,
manner: bare ablative licensed by the adjective, exactly the rule you inferred from
*summā cum laude*.

### C — refinements

**C3. `Litterae ā praetōre per servum ad senātum missae erant.`**

Two things.

*(a) The split is correct and it's the win of the set.* `ā praetōre` = the true agent;
`per servum` = the intermediary. I did not teach `per` + acc. for the go-between in the
lesson — you derived it and rendered it "via a slave," which is precisely right. That was
on my grading watch-list as "if he splits these correctly it's a first-principles win."
It was.

*(b) But move the agent.* You wrote "The letters **(written)** by the praetor had been
sent." There's only one verb in the sentence — `missae erant` — and `ā praetōre` is *its*
agent. Nothing is written; the praetor is the sender. Read it as: **"A letter had been
sent by the praetor to the senate through a slave."** The praetor sends, the slave
carries. Your insertion of "(written)" invents a second, unstated verb and hands the
agent to it, which quietly weakens the very agent/intermediary distinction you'd just
nailed.

*(c) Vocabulary:* `litterae` in the **plural** means *a letter, a dispatch* (one document).
`littera` singular means *a letter of the alphabet*. So "The letters" should be "A letter"
or "The dispatch." This is a real trap — Caesar and Cicero use plural `litterae` for a
single piece of correspondence constantly.

*(d) Tense: `missae erant` = "had been sent."* You got it. See the note below.

**C5. `dē mōribus maiōrum`** — `mōribus` is plural, so *the customs/ways* (plural) *of the
ancestors*. Your instinct to preserve **`mōs maiōrum`** is exactly right and shows good
ear: it is the fixed Roman term for ancestral custom as an unwritten constitutional
authority — roughly "the way things have always been done," invoked in the senate as a
binding argument. Keeping it untranslated is what a scholar would do. Also: you spotted
the interlocked `magnā cum cūrā` and read it without stumbling — that's the *summā cum
laude* pattern from the lesson, in the wild, one hour later.

**C1, C2, C4 — clean.** Three specific things worth naming:
- **C2 `cum hostibus pugnāverat` → "had fought *against*."** `cum` is formally the
  accompaniment preposition, but with verbs of fighting Latin uses it adversarially. You
  rendered the sense rather than the form. That's translation, not decoding.
- **C4 `dīcēbat` → "used to speak."** Iterative imperfect, and you reached for the English
  construction that carries it. Also `dē rē pūblicā` — 5th declension again, untaught,
  handled without comment.
- **C1 `Prīmā lūce`** → "at first light (dawn)." Time when, ablative, and you gave both a
  literal and an idiomatic rendering.

### Higher-level commentary

**1. The tense slip did NOT recur, and that resolves an open question.** In the lesson you
read `victus est` as "is defeated" — the third instance of a pattern (`fuit`→future,
`pōnet`→present). In this homework you produced `missae erant` → "had been sent",
`pugnāverat` → "had fought", `dīcēbat` → "used to speak", `vēnērunt` → "came" — every
tense correct, including a **pluperfect passive** built on the exact structure you'd
slipped on. Conclusion: the slip is **unfamiliar structure**, not weak tense knowledge,
and it clears as soon as the structure is named once. Your tense discipline is now a
strength, not a watch item.

**2. Your errors have migrated *downward* in the stack.** Both A-section misses are
declension-level (neuter nom=acc; gender of `diēs`), not syntax-level. Nothing on this
page shows confusion about what a preposition governs, what a bare-case idiom means, or
how agent differs from means. The week's actual topic is closed. What's exposed instead
is noun morphology — which is what Week 2 opens on (declensions 4–5), so the timing is
convenient rather than concerning.

**3. Watch: gender agreement across declension boundaries.** `illō diē` is the shape of
this — a 1st/2nd-declension modifier (`ille`) agreeing with a 5th-declension noun. Latin
adjectives and demonstratives don't take their endings *from* the noun; they take them
from the noun's **gender, number, and case**, in their own declension pattern. When the
noun is in an unfamiliar declension the temptation is to match the *sound* (`-a` with
`-ē`?), and the fix is to ask the three questions instead. `Rēs pūblica` is the friendly
version of this (both feminine, endings rhyme); `illō diē` and `magnus exercitus` are the
versions that bite.

**4. Flagging behaviour remains excellent.** Three self-flags on this page (the singular
in A2, the dative ruled out in B3, the `mōs maiōrum` note in C5), and all three were
correct. Continue — your uncertainty markers are reliable enough that I grade them as
evidence.
