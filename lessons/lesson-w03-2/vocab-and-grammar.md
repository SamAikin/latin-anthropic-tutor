# Lesson-w03-2 — Reference: Reading Participle Phrases

## The procedure (do it in this order, every time)

1. **Spot** the participle (yesterday's forms: -ns/-nt-, -tus/-sus, -ūrus).
2. **Find its noun**: same case, number, gender. Check ALL candidates — the nearest
   noun is not always the right one; the agreeing one is.
3. **Bracket** everything between (and including) the noun and participle. What's
   inside the bracket belongs to the participle: agents, objects, prepositional
   phrases.
4. **Read the skeleton** sentence around the bracket. Attach the bracket as a unit.

## Worked example (the diagnostic sentence)

`Consul litteras a Caesare missas legit.`

- Participle: missas (perf. pass., acc. f. pl.)
- Agreement: litteras (acc. f. pl.) ✓ — not consul (nom.), not Caesare (abl.)
- Bracket: Consul [litteras a Caesare missas] legit.
- Skeleton: Consul [X] legit → The consul read [the letters sent by Caesar].

The failure mode this kills: reading word-by-word left to right and letting English
order glue "consul + sent + letters + Caesar" into a new sentence. The bracket makes
the phrase a single object; there is nothing left to invert.

## The sandwich habit of Latin authors

Latin loves to open the noun early and close with the participle late:

`urbem [a militibus multos menses obsessam] ceperunt`
— the city, [besieged by the soldiers for many months], they took.

Everything inside the sandwich answers questions about the participle (by whom? how
long?). When lost mid-sentence, look FORWARD for the participle that closes a noun
you're still holding open.

## Three English registers for one bracket

`Naves [frumentum portantes] piratae ceperunt.`

1. Relative clause: "Pirates captured the ships **that were carrying** grain."
2. Participle: "Pirates captured the ships **carrying** grain."
3. New clause: "The ships were carrying grain, **and** pirates captured them."

All three show comprehension; pick whichever yields honest English. (Our standing
rule: the Latin comprehension is the target, not the English polish.)

## Deponent reminder inside brackets

`dux, [multa militibus pollicitus], signum dedit` — pollicitus is deponent: "having
PROMISED" (active), never "having been promised."
