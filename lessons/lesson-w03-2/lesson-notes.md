# Lesson-w03-2

## Primer

Yesterday you built participles; today you read them. The diagnostic failure mode was
precise: faced with `Consul litteras a Caesare missas legit`, you flipped the sentence
into "The consul sent letters to Caesar" — the participle phrase dissolved and its
pieces reattached themselves wherever English word order suggested. The cure is a
mechanical reading procedure: **find the agreement, bracket the phrase, then read the
sentence around the bracket.** Latin writers sandwich whole phrases between a noun and
its participle; once you see the sandwich, you can't unsee it.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept: the noun–participle sandwich and the bracketing reflex.

1. **Hook (5 min).** THE diagnostic sentence, on the table: `Consul litteras a
   Caesare missas legit.` Walk the failure openly (he inverted it). Now the
   procedure: litteras...missas agree (acc. f. pl.) → bracket [litteras a Caesare
   missas] → skeleton: Consul [X] legit. The consul read X. X = the letters-sent-by-
   Caesar. No inversion possible once bracketed.
2. **Bracketing drills (12 min).** Five sentences, increasing sandwich width. He
   brackets FIRST (out loud: "what agrees with what"), translates second. Include one
   distractor where two nouns could agree with the participle until case is checked
   carefully — the drill is checking, not guessing.
3. **Translation registers (8 min).** A bracketed phrase can surface in English as:
   relative clause ("the letters WHICH WERE SENT"), participle ("the letters SENT"),
   or — often best — a new clause ("Caesar sent letters, and the consul read them").
   Translate one sentence all three ways; discuss when each reads naturally. Reading
   comprehension first, English craft second (our standing rule).
4. **Wrap (5 min).** Homework (~30 min): bracket-then-translate set. Tomorrow: the
   ablative absolute — the sandwich that detaches from the sentence entirely.
