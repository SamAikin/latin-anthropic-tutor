This isn't a knowledge gap — you handled the vocative correctly elsewhere (B4 `Amici`,
C3 `Domine`). It's a write-it-all-out habit. Going forward, always lay down all six

### C — Translation (5/5 comprehension)

1. ✓ "The farmer's son carries water from the road." All five words placed correctly.
   - **Your `e`/`ex` guess is right.** They're one and the same preposition (+ ablative,
     "from / out of"). `ex` is used before vowels (and optionally before consonants);
     `e` only before consonants. `via` starts with `v`, so `e via`. Same alternation as
     `a`/`ab` and `neque`/`nec`. Good ear.
2. ✓ "The Roman people do not love the senate's plan." Clean. And note: `consilium` is
   the word you guessed as "consensus" in the lesson — here you rendered it correctly as
   "plan." The correction stuck.
3. ✓ "Lord, your servants do not work in the fields." `Domine` is the vocative of
   `dominus` (`-us` → `-e`) — the very ending you had reversed in the lesson. Reading it,
   you nailed it: direct address, "Lord/Master." The correction is holding.
4. Comprehension correct, **one refinement:** `bella` is neuter accusative **plural** —
   "wars," not "war." So: "The lack of grain causes **wars**." Worth sitting with the
   whole sentence's number logic: the subject `inopia` is singular, which is why the verb
   `facit` is singular ("*it* causes"); but the object `bella` is plural. Each ending
   tracks its own word — the singular verb doesn't make the plural object singular. You
   had `bella` as a plural cold in A2 and B7, so this is a slip under translation load,
   not a gap. "Want" for `inopia` is a good, precise choice.
5. ✓ "The daughters of the farmers carry gifts to the temple of the goddess." You asked
   me to be harsh — I can't be; it's right. You resolved four separate ambiguous endings
   correctly in one sentence: `Filiae` (nom. pl. subject), `agricolarum` (gen. pl.),
   `dona` (neut. acc. pl., "gifts"), `deae` (gen. sg., "of the goddess"), and kept
   `portant` plural. Note you handled `dona` as a neuter plural here — which is why C4's
   `bella` is clearly just a slip, not a pattern.
