# Lesson-w03-4

## Primer

The diagnostic showed `pugnavisse` meant nothing to you — a perfect infinitive,
invisible because school Latin usually teaches one infinitive and moves on. Latin has
six (three tenses × two voices), and they are the entire skeleton of indirect
statement, which is week 4's centerpiece and the single most common construction in
Latin historical prose. Today: build all six, see where infinitives already live in
your Latin (complementary: potest venire), and end with a 5-minute preview that makes
next week feel inevitable rather than new.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept: the infinitive system — six forms, one skeleton.

1. **Hook (4 min).** He knows venire ("to come"). Ask: how would Latin say "to have
   come"? (venisse — the diagnostic ghost pugnavisse gets named here and dies here.)
2. **The 2×3 grid (12 min).** Build from amo and capio:
   - present: amare / amari; capere / capi (3rd-conj. passive in -i, flag it)
   - perfect: amavisse / amatus esse; cepisse / captus esse
   - future: amaturus esse / (supine + iri — name only, park it)
   Recipes: perfect active = 3rd principal part + -isse; perfect passive = participle
   + esse (the week's machine again); future active = -urus participle + esse.
   Deponents: one column only, active meanings (secutus esse, conari, conatus esse).
3. **Where infinitives live (8 min).** Complementary (possum, volo, debeo, conor +
   inf — he's been reading these for weeks); objective (iubeo te venire); subject
   (errare humanum est). Rapid parse: cepisse, mitti, laturus esse, passus esse,
   venisse, defendi.
4. **Preview + wrap (6 min).** ONE indirect statement, no theory: `Scio milites
   venisse.` — "I know the soldiers came." Notice: accusative + infinitive after a
   knowing-verb; tense of infinitive relative to scio. That's the whole of next
   week's machine, seen once, unexplained. Homework (~25 min). Week 3 done — the
   participle week. Readings w03-5 and w03-6 will now read differently than they
   would have on Monday.
