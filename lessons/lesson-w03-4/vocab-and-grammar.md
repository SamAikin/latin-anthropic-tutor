# Lesson-w03-4 — Reference: The Infinitive System

## The grid (capiō)

| | Active | Passive |
|---|---|---|
| Present | capere — "to take" | capī — "to be taken" |
| Perfect | cēpisse — "to have taken" | captus esse — "to have been taken" |
| Future | captūrus esse — "to be about to take" | (captum īrī — name only; rare, parked) |

## Manufacturing recipes

| Infinitive | Recipe | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| pres. act. | 2nd principal part | vocāre, tenēre, dūcere, venīre |
| pres. pass. | -re → -rī (1/2/4); -ere → -ī (3rd!) | vocārī, tenērī, dūcī, capī, audīrī |
| perf. act. | 3rd principal part + -isse | vocāvisse, cēpisse, tulisse, vēnisse |
| perf. pass. | perf. participle + esse | vocātus esse, captus esse, missus esse |
| fut. act. | -ūrus participle + esse | vocātūrus esse, captūrus esse, futūrus esse |

Watch-out: the 3rd-conjugation present passive is a bare -ī (dūcī, mittī, capī) — easy
to mistake for something else; when a lone short form won't parse, try "to be X-ed."

Irregulars worth owning now: esse/fuisse (sum); posse/potuisse (possum); ferre/ferrī/
tulisse (ferō); velle/voluisse (volō); īre/īsse (eō).

Deponents (active meanings, passive-shaped forms): cōnārī (to try), cōnātus esse (to
have tried); sequī, secūtus esse; patī, passus esse; morī, mortuus esse.

## Where infinitives live (before indirect statement)

| Use | Example | English |
|---|---|---|
| complementary | mīlitēs trānsīre nōn poterant | the soldiers could not cross |
| objective | cōnsul urbem dēfendī iussit | the consul ordered the city to be defended |
| subject | errāre hūmānum est / dulce est prō patriā morī | to err is human / it is sweet to die for one's country |

## The tense principle (same as participles — RELATIVE)

| Infinitive | Time relative to the governing verb |
|---|---|
| present (capere) | same time |
| perfect (cēpisse) | before |
| future (captūrus esse) | after |

## Preview of week 4 (one sentence, no theory)

`Sciō mīlitēs vēnisse.` — I know the soldiers came.
Accusative subject (mīlitēs) + infinitive (vēnisse, perfect = before the knowing).
Every verb of saying, thinking, knowing, and perceiving in Latin runs reported speech
through exactly this frame. That is week 4.
