# Lesson-w04-1

## Primer

The last stretch of the rust line. In the diagnostic, `Caesar dixit milites fortiter
pugnavisse` defeated you — you guessed at a subjunctive fear clause and produced
"should fight harder." The real machine is simpler and vastly more common: **indirect
statement** — after any verb of saying, thinking, knowing, or perceiving, Latin drops
the reported sentence into accusative + infinitive. No conjunction ("that" doesn't
exist here), no subjunctive. You built all six infinitives last week; today they start
carrying freight. This construction is on nearly every page of Caesar, Cicero, Nepos,
and Sallust — it is the single highest-value construction left between you and real
prose.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept: acc + inf after head verbs.

1. **Hook (5 min).** Direct: `Milites venerunt.` Now put "Caesar says" in front of it.
   English: adds "that," keeps the sentence intact. Latin: `Caesar dicit milites
   venisse.` — subject drops to accusative, verb becomes infinitive. One
   transformation, learned once, reused forever.
2. **The transformation drill (12 min).** He converts direct → indirect after dico/
   scio/puto: `Consul urbem defendit.` → `Scio consulem urbem defendere.`; `Hostes
   fugerunt.` → `Puto hostes fugisse.` Then decode direction: given indirect, restore
   the direct statement. Both directions until the reflex fires. Flag the two-
   accusative hazard (`Scio consulem hostes vicisse` — subject vs object: word order
   and sense decide; authors keep it readable).
3. **The diagnostic sentence, retired (8 min).** `Caesar dixit milites fortiter
   pugnavisse.` He now has every part: dixit (head verb), milites (acc subject),
   pugnavisse (perf. inf. = before the saying), fortiter (adverb: "bravely," not
   "harder"). Translate, compare to his diagnostic guess, enjoy the distance. Then
   two more from this week's readings (w04-1 Nepos: `vidit civitatem esse
   perturbatam`).
4. **Wrap (5 min).** Head-verb families (dico/nego, scio, puto, video, audio,
   intellego — the week's vocab theme, deliberately). Homework (~30 min). Tomorrow:
   the tense-of-infinitive logic in full, and se vs eum.
