# Lesson-w04-1 — Reference: Indirect Statement I

## The transformation

Direct: `Mīlitēs vēnērunt.` (The soldiers came.)
Indirect: `Caesar dīcit **mīlitēs vēnisse**.` (Caesar says that the soldiers came.)

1. Subject: nominative → **accusative**
2. Verb: finite → **infinitive**
3. No conjunction. Latin has no "that" here — the acc+inf IS the that-clause.

## Head verbs (the triggers)

Any verb of saying, thinking, knowing, perceiving, reporting:

| Family | Verbs (week-4 vocab, mostly) |
|---|---|
| saying | dīcō, negō, respondeō, nūntiō, narrō, prōmittō |
| thinking | putō, crēdō, arbitror, exīstimō, spērō |
| knowing | sciō, intellegō, cognōscō |
| perceiving | videō, audiō, sentiō |

**negō eats the "not":** `Negat mīlitēs vēnisse` = "He says the soldiers did NOT
come." Latin prefers negō + positive infinitive over dīcō + nōn.

## Reading procedure

1. Meet a head verb → expect acc + inf.
2. Find the accusative that acts as subject.
3. Find the infinitive; its tense is RELATIVE to the head verb (full logic tomorrow;
   for today: present inf = same time, perfect inf = before).
4. Translate as an English that-clause.

Two-accusative hazard: `Sciō cōnsulem hostēs vīcisse` — consul (subject) defeated the
enemy (object). Word order usually puts the subject first; sense confirms.

## Worked examples

- `Caesar dīxit mīlitēs fortiter pugnāvisse.`
  dīxit → head verb. mīlitēs → acc subject. pugnāvisse → perfect infinitive (before
  the saying). fortiter → adverb, "bravely."
  = "Caesar said that the soldiers had fought bravely." (The diagnostic sentence —
  retired with honors.)

- `Vīdit cīvitātem esse perturbātam.` (Nepos, Atticus 2 — this week's reading w04-1)
  vīdit → head verb. cīvitātem → acc subject. esse perturbātam → perfect passive
  infinitive. = "He saw that the state had been thrown into confusion."

- `Omnēs intellēxērunt eum sē ipsum damnāvisse.` (reading w04-3)
  = "Everyone understood that he had condemned himself." (sē — tomorrow's topic —
  points back at eum.)

## What indirect statement is NOT

- Not a subjunctive construction (your diagnostic guess — a natural one; fear clauses
  exist, but they follow verbs of fearing, not saying).
- Not introduced by ut, nē, quod, or quia in classical prose. Acc + inf, always,
  after these verbs.
