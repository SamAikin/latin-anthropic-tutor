# Lesson-w03-3

## Primer

In the diagnostic you looked at `Urbe capta, cives fugerunt`, correctly NAMED it an
ablative absolute — and then translated it as a future conditional. Today closes that
gap for good. The ablative absolute is yesterday's noun–participle sandwich with one
twist: it's grammatically disconnected from the rest of the sentence (that's the
"absolute" — absolutus, untied). Latin uses it where English uses a subordinate
clause: "after the city was taken," "with the city taken," "once the city had been
taken." Your week-3 readings are already full of them — including two in Eutropius's
own words (reading w03-5) that you may already have met.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept: the detached ablative sandwich.

1. **Hook (5 min).** THE sentence: `Urbe capta, cives fugerunt.` Walk his diagnostic
   answer honestly: he named the construction, then read it as "if the city is
   captured... will flee." Two fixes: (a) capta is PERFECT participle = before the
   main verb; (b) fugerunt is perfect = "fled." Literal scaffold once: "the city
   having-been-captured, the citizens fled" — then never again; idiomatic from here.
2. **The pattern (10 min).** Noun + participle, both ablative, comma-fenced, no
   grammatical link to the main clause (the noun is NOT the subject or object).
   Translation menu: after/when/because/although X — context picks the flavor. Drill
   recognition against yesterday's attached sandwiches: `urbem captam incenderunt`
   (attached — object) vs `urbe capta, discesserunt` (absolute). He sorts five.
3. **Variants (8 min).** Present participle: `Caesare veniente` (with Caesar coming —
   same time). No-participle type: `Cicerone consule` (with Cicero as consul — dating
   formula, ubiquitous); `Sulla dictatore`. From Eutropius (this week's reading):
   `effracto Capuae ludo` and `multis ducibus victis` — real wild specimens on the
   table.
4. **Wrap (7 min).** Live translation of three, then homework preview (~30 min).
   Tomorrow: the infinitive system — last piece before indirect statement.
