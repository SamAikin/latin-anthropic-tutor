# Lesson-w04-2 — Reference: Indirect Statement II

## Relative tense — the full logic

The infinitive states time RELATIVE to the head verb:

| Infinitive | Relation | After present head (dīcit) | After past head (dīxit) |
|---|---|---|---|
| present (venīre) | same time | says they ARE coming | said they WERE coming |
| perfect (vēnisse) | before | says they CAME | said they HAD come |
| future (ventūrōs esse) | after | says they WILL come | said they WOULD come |

The English shifts; the Latin doesn't. When translating, first fix the head verb's
time, then place the infinitive relative to it.

Passive infinitives run the same clock: `dīxit urbem capī` (was being taken — same
time), `dīxit urbem captam esse` (had been taken — before).

Agreement note: in perfect-passive and future-active infinitives the participle
agrees with the ACCUSATIVE subject: `dīxit urbem captam esse`, `dīxit mīlitēs
ventūrōs esse`, `dīxit lēgiōnēs ventūrās esse`.

## se vs eum — who is "he"?

Inside indirect statement:

| Word | Points to |
|---|---|
| sē / suus | the REPORTER (subject of the head verb) |
| eum, eam, eōs / eius, eōrum | someone ELSE |

- `Caesar dīxit **sē** ventūrum esse.` — Caesar said that HE (Caesar) would come.
- `Caesar dīxit **eum** ventūrum esse.` — Caesar said that HE (another man) would come.
- `Rēx dīxit sē cōpiās **suās** missūrum esse.` — his own troops (the king's).
- `Rēx dīxit sē cōpiās **eius** missūrum esse.` — that other man's troops.

English "he/his" flattens this distinction; Latin never does. When you meet sē in a
reading, resolve it to the reporter before translating.

## Specimens from this week's readings

- `Catilīna dīcēbat **sē** tabulās novās factūrum esse` (w04-6) — Catiline kept
  saying that HE HIMSELF would enact a debt cancellation. (se → Catiline; future
  inf. → after his saying.)
- `Cicerō putābat **sē** patriam servāvisse` (w04-6) — Cicero thought that he himself
  HAD SAVED the fatherland. (perfect inf. → before the thinking.)
- `dīcēbat sē nōn ā maiōribus sed ā **sē ipsō** nōmen suum accēpisse` (w04-3) — the
  reflexive doing double duty, and proudly.

## negō, revisited

`Negāvit sē quicquam vīdisse.` = "He said that he had seen nothing." negō + positive
infinitive; the negative lives in the head verb. (quisquam appears precisely because
the sentence is negative in sense — week 2 vocab, full circle.)
