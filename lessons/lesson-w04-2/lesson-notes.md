# Lesson-w04-2

## Primer

Yesterday installed the frame; today installs the two precision parts. First: the
infinitive's tense is **relative to the head verb**, not absolute — `dixit milites
venire` means the coming was happening WHEN he spoke (so English: "said they WERE
coming"). Getting this wrong shifts every reported timeline in a historian by one
notch, which is how misreadings of whole paragraphs start. Second: inside reported
speech, Latin distinguishes "himself" (se — same person as the speaker) from "him"
(eum — somebody else) with total precision, where English "he" is ambiguous. Cicero's
prosecutions turn on this distinction; so does every page of Caesar.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept (two facets): relative tense; se vs eum.

1. **Hook (5 min).** Triplet on the table, same head verb:
   `Dixit milites venire / venisse / venturos esse.`
   Was-coming / had-come / would-come. One head verb, three timelines — the
   infinitive alone moves the clock. Have him assign the English to each.
2. **Relative tense drills (10 min).** Grid practice: head verb past vs present ×
   infinitive tense. `Scit urbem capi` (knows it IS being taken) vs `Scivit urbem
   capi` (knew it WAS being taken) — the present infinitive slides with the head
   verb. Then perfect and future infinitives on both head-verb tenses. Six decode
   reps, out loud, fast.
3. **se vs eum (10 min).** `Caesar dixit se venturum esse` (Caesar himself will come)
   vs `Caesar dixit eum venturum esse` (someone else will). Drill with paired
   scenarios; include suus vs eius (`dixit se copias suas missurum esse` — whose
   troops?). The rule: se/suus reach back to the REPORTER; is/eius point elsewhere.
   One real specimen: `Catilina dicebat se tabulas novas facturum esse` (reading
   w04-6).
4. **Wrap (5 min).** Homework (~30 min). Tomorrow: conditions — including the
   pluperfect subjunctive that ambushed you in the diagnostic (`Si hoc scivissem...`),
   handled recognition-first.
