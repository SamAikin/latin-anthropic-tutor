### Section A ‚Äî 10/12 forms

**A1 `mitto`, 3rd plural ‚Äî perfect, all six.** Including `mittent` (3rd-conj. future,
the same `-e-` future you missed on `ponet` yesterday) and the `miserunt` /
`miserint` perfect-vs-future-perfect distinction, which is carried by one vowel.

**A2 `teneo`, 1st singular ‚Äî 4/6, and the two misses are the same miss twice:**

| tense | your answer | correct |
|---|---|---|
| future | ~~tenebim~~ | **tenebo** |
| future perfect | ~~tenuerim~~ | **tenuero** |

Here's what happened. I taught you the tense signals `-bi-` (future) and `-eri-`
(future perfect), and you applied them faithfully ‚Äî but **the 1st person singular is
exactly the one slot where both signals contract.** The full endings are:

> future (1st/2nd conj.): **-bo**, -bis, -bit, -bimus, -bitis, -bunt
> future perfect (all verbs): **-ero**, -eris, -erit, -erimus, -eritis, -erint

The `-i-` of `-bi-` and `-eri-` disappears before the 1st-sg. `-o`: `tenebo`, not
`tenebim`; `tenuero`, not `tenuerim`. Notice you got `miserint` (3rd pl.) right in A1
‚Äî the signal works everywhere *except* the slot you happened to draw in A2. That's
partly my teaching artifact: I gave you the signal without flagging where it bends.
Now you have the full rows.

(Filed for much later: `tenuerim` is actually a real Latin form ‚Äî the perfect
*subjunctive*. The subjunctive lives one vowel away from the future perfect through
most of the paradigm, which is exactly why the 1st sg. `-o` vs. `-im` distinction
exists. Not this month's problem.)

### Section B ‚Äî parsing 8/8; principal parts are the real finding

**First, what was asked: tense, person, number, meaning. On those, you went eight
for eight.** Every single tense identification is correct, including both designed
traps (`petent` and `creabit` read as futures ‚Äî see headline) and `reliquerint` as a
future perfect. That layer is done.

**But your dictionary forms contained errors in five of the eight items** ‚Äî and the
errors have a consistent shape:

| # | your version | correct |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | gessi, ~~gessum~~ | gessi, **gestum** |
| 3 | ~~reliqo, reliqere~~, reliqui, ~~reliquum~~ | **relinquo, relinquere**, reliqui, **relictum** |
| 4 | venire, ~~vensi, vensum~~ | venire, **veni, ventum** |
| 5 | tuli, ~~tulum~~ | tuli, **latum** |
| 7 | possum, ~~potere~~, potui | possum, **posse**, potui |

Look at what you did in each case: **you reconstructed the parts you didn't have by
extending a pattern from the parts you did have.** `gessi` ‚Üí "so probably gessum."
`reliqui` ‚Üí "so the present must be reliqo." `tuli` ‚Üí "so tulum." That is exactly
the right instinct for regular verbs ‚Äî and these are precisely the verbs where it
fails, because irregular principal parts are *facts*, not derivations:

- **`relinquo`** keeps a nasal `-n-` in the whole present system (`relinquo,
  relinquere`) and *drops* it in the perfect (`reliqui`). You worked backwards from
  the perfect and un-nasaled the present. The 4th part is `relictum`.
- **`venio`'s** perfect is just `veni` ‚Äî a lengthened stem vowel, no `-s-`. (`vensi`
  guesses a sigmatic perfect like `gessi`/`mansi`; venio isn't one.)
- **`fero`** builds its parts from three unrelated roots: `fer-`, `tul-`, `lat-`.
  The 4th part is `latum` ‚Äî which you'll meet constantly, because it's inside
  `translatum`, `relatum`, `illatum`, i.e. English *translate*, *relate*, *inflate*.
- **`possum`** is a compound of `potis` + `sum`, so its infinitive is `posse`
  (pot- + esse). `potere` doesn't exist.
- **`gero`**'s 4th part is `gestum` (cf. English *gesture*, *digest*).

Why I'm dwelling on this rather than waving it through: **the 4th principal part is
the factory for week 3.** Participles ‚Äî the promissory note from Monday's lesson ‚Äî
are built directly on it. `gestum`, `relictum`, `ventum`, `latum` are not trivia
rows; in two weeks they become "having been waged," "abandoned," etc. So the parts
need to be *recalled*, not *derived*. When you type verb cards into Anki, say all
four parts; I'll also seed irregular-parts verbs into future synopses.

**Meaning glosses, two small notes:**

- **B5 `tulerunt` ‚Üí "they cast (as in their ballot)":** I love what this shows ‚Äî
  `suffragium ferre` from yesterday's reading has genuinely stuck. But "cast" is
  what `fero` means *inside that idiom*. The bare form's core meaning is **"they
  carried / bore / brought."** In a parse exercise, give the core sense first, then
  the idiom if you want the bonus point (you'd have gotten it).
- **B8 `egit` ‚Üí "he made/caused":** `ago` is **do, drive, conduct** ‚Äî "he did," "he
  drove." *Make* belongs to `facio`, *cause* to `efficio`. `ago` is the verb of
  keeping something in motion (`vitam agere` = to lead a life, `gratias agere` = to
  give thanks) ‚Äî the root of English *agent* and *act*, not *make*.
