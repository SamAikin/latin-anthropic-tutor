# Lesson-w01-3

## Primer

The diagnostic showed your tense system is largely alive: you read present, imperfect,
perfect, and even an irregular future correctly, and your explanation of perfect-vs-
imperfect aspect was textbook quality. Today we make the whole active indicative
explicit as ONE system — six tenses built from two stems — and shore up the two corners
you weren't tested on: pluperfect and future perfect. The organizing idea: **principal
parts are not trivia; they are the machine.** Everything this month (including week 3's
participles) is built from them.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept: six tenses, two stems, one system.

1. **Hook (4 min).** capiō, capere, cēpī, captum — four forms, ask: why does the
   dictionary "waste" four slots per verb? Draw out: each part is a factory for other
   forms. Parts 1–2 → present stem; part 3 → perfect stem; part 4 → week 3's payoff
   (participles), name it now as a promissory note.
2. **The map (10 min).** Present system (present, imperfect, future) on the present
   stem; perfect system (perfect, pluperfect, future perfect) on the perfect stem.
   Signals: -bā- (impf), -bi-/-ē- (fut, by conjugation), -erā- (plupf), -eri- (futpf).
   He articulated perfect-vs-imperfect aspect in the diagnostic — have him extend the
   logic: pluperfect = "past before the past," future perfect = "done before a future
   point." Use his own diagnostic sentence (ceperunt/dabat) as the anchor.
3. **Synopsis practice (12 min).** Synopsis = one verb, one person/number, all six
   tenses. Do dūcō in 3rd sg. together; then he does mittō in 3rd pl. and vocō in 1st
   sg. with decreasing help. Then reverse: parse mīserat, cēperint, dabit, vēnerant.
   Irregulars: quick recognition pass on sum/possum/ferō/volō (est/erat/fuit/erit,
   potest/potuit, fert/tulit, vult/voluit) — recognition, not production.
4. **Wrap (4 min).** Homework preview (~30 min): two synopses + parsing + translation.
   Tomorrow closes week 1 with prepositions and case idioms.

Speed-run discipline: if the future-perfect drags, note it and move — the readings
use it sparingly and it will recur.
