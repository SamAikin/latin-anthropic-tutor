### Section A — 11/12 form-blocks

**`civis` — perfect.** All six cases, both numbers, correctly labeled. Including the one
form most people get wrong: **abl. sg. `cive`, not `civī`.** Hold that thought; it is
about to become the most important thing in this evaluation.

**`corpus` — one error.**

| | your answer | correct |
|---|---|---|
| abl. sg. | ~~corporī~~ | **corpore** |

Everything else was right, including `corporum` (gen. pl.) and `corpora` (nom./acc./voc.
pl.). You put the *dative* form in the ablative slot.

**Answering your bracketed note directly, because you were closer than you knew.** You
wrote that you second-guessed `corpora` vs. `corporia`, and that i-stems "you only know
them when you see them" has thrown you. Here is the rule that fixes it — and it also
fixes the error above:

> **The neuter i-stem markers are a package deal.** Abl. sg. **-ī** and nom./acc. pl.
> **-ia** travel *together*. A neuter noun either gets **both** (`mare` → `marī`, `maria`)
> or **neither** (`corpus` → `corpore`, `corpora`).

You *correctly rejected* `corporia`. That rejection already told you `corporī` was
impossible — you just didn't cash the inference. If it isn't `corporia`, it can't be
`corporī`. One decision, not two.

And the second half of the rule, which explains why `cive` was right:

> **Masculine/feminine i-stems** (`civis`, `urbs`, `hostis`, `pars`) show their
> i-stem-ness in **exactly one place: the genitive plural -ium.** Their ablative
> singular is a perfectly ordinary **-e**. `civis → cive, civium`. `urbs → urbe, urbium`.

So: the *only* thing the i-stem label buys you for a masc./fem. noun is `-ium`. Nothing
else changes. That is the entire footprint.

**B3 deserves real credit before we get to the misses.** `tempora omnia` — a
declension-3 adjective on a declension-3 neuter noun, and the endings *don't match*
(`-a` vs. `-ia`), and you built it right anyway. That is the `gravia bella` insight from

**Now the one mistake wearing four hats.** Look at B2 and B4 next to A's `corporī`:

- `militium` — you added an **-i-** that doesn't belong (should be `militum`)
- `urbi` — you used **-ī** for an ablative that takes `-e` (should be `urbe`)
- `corporī` — same thing again (should be `corpore`)

**You are over-applying the i-stem markers.** In the lesson I taught i-stems as
*recognition-only* — "when you meet `civium`, read 'of the citizens' and move on." That
was the right call for *reading*, and your reading is fine. But this homework asked you
to **produce** the forms, and production exposed what recognition hid: the `-i-` has
leaked out of its box and is now colouring forms that never had one.

That is my sequencing error as much as your morphology error. So here is the box, drawn
tight. **The i-stem `-i-` appears in exactly three places, and nowhere else:**

1. **gen. pl. `-ium`** — for i-stems only (`civium`, `urbium`, `hostium`, `partium`)
2. **abl. sg. `-ī`** — for **neuter** i-stems only (`marī`)
3. **nom./acc. pl. `-ia`** — for **neuter** i-stems only (`maria`)

Everything outside those three slots is a plain declension-3 ending. And the test for
membership is the one you already have: **`miles, mīlitis`** — the genitive plural is
`mīlitum`, and you knew that in the lesson (you contrasted `hostium` vs. `mīlitum`
correctly and unprompted). Under production load, it regressed. Not a knowledge gap —
a stability problem. It will settle with exposure.

**B5 is a different and more serious bug, so don't let it hide behind the i-stem story.**
`homoni` is not a Latin form. The stem of `homo` is **`homin-`**, and you get that from
the **genitive singular `hominis`** — which is the very first rule of declension 3 and
the entire reason the dictionary hands you a second form. You took the *nominative*
`homo` and bolted an ending onto it. Do that with `rex` and you get `regi`... which
happens to be right by accident, but do it with `corpus`, `tempus`, `iter`, or `homo`
and it collapses. **Always build from the genitive.** (`ingentī` was correct, by the way
— the adjective was fine.)

**B6:** `marī` was *right*, and right for the right reason — neuter i-stem, abl. sg. `-ī`.
Then `nostri` broke agreement. `noster, nostra, nostrum` is a 2-1-2 adjective; `mare` is
**neuter**; ablative singular neuter is **`nostrō`**. `nostrī` is genitive. So: **`marī
nostrō`** — which, as you spotted, is *mare nostrum* in the nominative, the Roman name
for the Mediterranean. Good cultural catch; the case just slipped.

**Vocabulary, minor but worth naming.** Your glosses scrambled three adjectives:

- `magnus` = **great, large** (you wrote "strong city" in B4 — but "great" in C4, so it's a slip)
- `ingens` = **huge, enormous** (you wrote "brave man" in B5)
- `fortis` = **brave, strong** (correct in B2)

C4 proves you know `magnus`. These are load slips, not gaps — but `ingens` → "brave"
suggests `fortis` is bleeding into it. Keep them distinct.

**Your convention question — answered, and it's a good question.** You asked how to
represent the "meaning" of an isolated cased phrase when the function is context-dependent.
You're right that it is, and the honest answer is: **gloss the lexical meaning, name the
case, and do not guess the function.** So for B4 I want:

> `urbe magna` — "(a/the) great city," abl. sg.

Not "of the great city" or "in the great city" — there's no sentence, so there's no

sentence. **New standing convention: bare phrase → lexical meaning + case label. That's it.**
