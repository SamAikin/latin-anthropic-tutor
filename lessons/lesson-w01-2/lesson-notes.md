# Lesson-w01-2

## Primer

Declension 3 is where most Latin nouns actually live — rex, urbs, miles, homo, tempus —
and the diagnostic showed you already read its common forms in context. Today makes the
system explicit (including i-stems, which you likely never consciously learned), and
adds adjective agreement: how 2-1-2 adjectives (magnus) and 3rd-declension adjectives
(fortis) attach to nouns of *any* declension. The key insight to arrive with: agreement
is case–number–gender, **not** matching endings. `fortis puella` and `militum omnium`
are both perfect agreement with zero ending-match.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept: declension 3 + agreement across declensions.

1. **Hook (4 min).** `Urbem milites capiunt.` He read this pattern in the diagnostic.
   Socratic: what case is urbem, what gives it away, what's the nominative? Then the
   catch: the genitive plural — urbium, not urbum. Why? I-stems exist.
2. **Core paradigms (10 min).** rex (m/f pattern), corpus/tempus (neuter), from the
   Reference. Then i-stems: urbs, civis, mare — the practical rule of thumb (gen. pl.
   -ium; neuters in -e/-al/-ar also abl. sg. -ī, n. pl. -ia). Keep it recognition-first:
   for READING, the only high-frequency difference is -ium and -ia.
3. **Agreement (12 min).** magnus -a -um across a decl-3 noun: magnus rex, magnae
   urbis, magnis militibus. Then 3rd-decl adjectives: fortis/forte, omnis/omne, ingens.
   Drill mixed pairs both directions ("make 'all the laws' accusative"; "parse omnium
   temporum"). Emphasize: the adjective can sit far from its noun — agreement is the
   thread that ties them. This is the skill week 3's participles will stand on.
4. **Wrap (4 min).** Homework preview (~30 min). Note the arc: nouns done in two days;
   verbs tomorrow.

If i-stems cause friction, do not drill the sub-rules — flag as recognition-only and
move on. Speed run: agreement matters more than i-stem taxonomy.

**The headline finding — one bug, five symptoms: the i-stem `-i-` is escaping its box
in PRODUCTION.**
- `militium` for `militum` (B2) — added -i- to a non-i-stem
- `urbi` for `urbe` (B4) — used -ī for a masc./fem. i-stem ablative that takes -e

He knows the box exists — he got `cive` right (the form most learners miss), got `marī`
right for the right reason, and in the lesson he *himself* contrasted `hostium` vs.
`militum` correctly and unprompted. Under production load it regressed. Taught the fix
as two tight rules: (1) neuter i-stem markers are a **package** — abl. sg. -ī and
nom./acc. pl. -ia travel together, so rejecting `corporia` already disproves `corporī`
(he rejected `corporia` in his own bracketed note and failed to cash the inference);
(2) masc./fem. i-stems show it in **exactly one slot**, gen. pl. -ium — everything else
is a plain decl-3 ending.

**Root cause is partly mine.** I taught i-stems recognition-only ("don't memorize the
classification") — correct for reading, and his reading is clean. But the homework asked
for **production**, which recognition-first never prepared. Same class of sequencing
error as putting futures in reading w01-1. **Planning note: don't test in a mode the

**Separate, more serious, don't let it hide behind the i-stem story:** `homoni` (B5) —
built the dative off the *nominative* `homo` instead of the genitive stem `homin-`. That

**Also:** `nostri` for `nostrō` (B6) — agreement break on a neuter ablative, right after

C4 proves he knows `magnus`, so slips not gaps, but `fortis` appears to be bleeding into
`ingens`.

**Retention wins (same-day, again):** `hostium` → "of the enemies" in both B2 and C2,

`tempora omnia` (B3) built cold — decl-3 adjective on a decl-3 neuter noun with
non-matching endings. The lesson's core insight is in.

**Standing convention established** (he asked, good question): for a bare cased phrase
with no sentence, gloss **lexical meaning + case label only** — do not speculate about

**The through-line, now visible across three assessments: comprehension runs well ahead
