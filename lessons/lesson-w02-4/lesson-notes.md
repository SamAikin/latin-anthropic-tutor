# Lesson-w02-4

## Primer

Deponents are the payoff for yesterday's -r endings: a large family of common verbs —
sequor, loquor, utor, patior, conor, proficiscor — that wear passive form with active
meaning. There is no trick to them beyond that one sentence; the work is making the
reflex automatic, because they are everywhere in real prose (this week's readings use
them from w02-4 on, and Nepos will use them constantly in weeks 3–4). One diagnostic
callback to enjoy: you once guessed `venissem` as "fear" — likely a collision with
vereor. Today vereor gets properly introduced, and that ghost gets exorcised.

## Lesson Plan

Target 30 minutes. One concept: passive form, active meaning.

1. **Hook (4 min).** `Milites ducem sequuntur.` Passive ending, but who follows whom?
   There's no passive reading available — the object is right there in the accusative.
   Define deponent: laid aside (deponere) its active forms; keeps active meaning.
2. **The family tour (10 min).** This week's vocab deponents, grouped: 1st conj.
   (conor, hortor, arbitror, miror), 2nd (vereor, polliceor, fateor), 3rd (sequor,
   loquor, utor, patior, queror, nascor, morior, proficiscor), 4th (orior). He reads
   principal parts aloud for a few: conor, conari, conatus sum — the perfect is the
   participle machine again. utor takes the ablative (utor gladio) — flag as the one
   oddball case habit.
3. **Reflex drills (12 min).** Mixed parse-and-translate: sequitur, locutus est,
   conabantur, pollicetur, passi sunt, proficiscetur — for each: deponent or true
   passive? (Mix in real passives: capitur, missus est.) The discrimination rule:
   check the dictionary form. Then three short sentences translated live.
4. **Wrap (4 min).** Semi-deponents get one minute (audeo, gaudeo, soleo — active
   present system, deponent perfect: ausus est). Homework preview (~25 min). Week 2
   done — next week: the participle system, where the 4th principal part finally
   pays off in full.
