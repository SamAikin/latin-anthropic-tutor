# Lesson-w02-4 — Reference: Deponent Verbs

## The one rule

**Deponents have passive forms and active meanings.** sequitur = "he follows" (never
"he is followed"). They have no true passive.

Dictionary signature: passive-looking principal parts — sequor, sequī, secūtus sum.
When you meet an -r form, check the dictionary form: if the lemma itself is -or with
an -ī infinitive, it's deponent → translate actively.

## Forms in practice (sequor, 3rd sg.)

| Tense | Form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| present | sequitur | follows |
| imperfect | sequēbātur | was following |
| future | sequētur | will follow |
| perfect | secūtus est | followed |
| pluperfect | secūtus erat | had followed |

The perfect system uses the participle + esse, agreeing with the subject — exactly the
machine from yesterday, meaning active: `mīlitēs secūtī sunt` = the soldiers followed.

## Semi-deponents (recognition only)

Active present system, deponent perfect system: audeō, audēre, **ausus sum** (dare);
gaudeō, gaudēre, **gāvīsus sum** (rejoice); soleō, solēre, **solitus sum** (be
accustomed).

## Ghost-exorcism corner

| | veniō | vereor |
|---|---|---|
| infinitive | venīre (come) | verērī (fear) |
| perfect | vēnī | veritus sum |

`venissem` = "I had come" (subjunctive — week 4 territory), from veniō. Not fear.
Never was fear.
